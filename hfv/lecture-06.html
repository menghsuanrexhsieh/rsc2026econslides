Interventions · Rationale

Why Intervene in Markets?

A competitive market is efficient only when a big assumption holds: no market failures. This deck examines the two that break it:

  1. No externalities: producers must bear all production costs, and buyers must receive all the benefits. Wildfire smoke and pollution violate this.
  2. Everything is priced: if you can’t charge non-payers, no firm will supply the good. Clean air and other public goods violate this.

When either assumption fails, the market misfires on its own — and government intervention may be economically justified.

Interventions · Price Controls

Price Ceilings & Price Floors

Definition
Price controls are legislation that guarantees market prices — preventing prices from moving to equilibrium.
  • Price Ceiling (\( P_c \)): Legal maximum price. Binding only if below equilibrium (\( P_c < P^* \)), causing a shortage.
  • Price Floor (\( P_f \)): Legal minimum price. Binding only if above equilibrium (\( P_f > P^* \)), causing a surplus.

At any binding price control, the quantity actually transacted is always the minimum of \( Q_s \) and \( Q_d \).

Interventions · Taxes

Prices Controlled by Taxes

How do taxes affect market outcomes?

  • Governments artificially change transaction prices by introducing per-unit or percentage taxes.
  • A tax creates a wedge between what consumers pay (\( P_b \)) and what sellers receive (\( P_s \)).
  • This tax wedge restricts the quantity traded and creates deadweight loss.
Tax Identity
\[ P_b = P_s + t \] where \( t \) is the per-unit tax rate.

Interventions · Taxes

Anatomy of a Tax: Wedge, Revenue & DWL

A market at equilibrium \( (Q^* = 40,\ P^* = \$3) \) meets a big \( \$3 \) per-unit tax — drawn large (not to scale) so every piece shows. Press → to add each piece.

Tax wedge \( t = P_b - P_s \) ⇒ quantity falls ⇒ the trades between \( Q_{\text{tax}} \) and \( Q^* \) vanish. That lost surplus is the DWL triangle.

Interventions · Taxes

Careful With Words: Remit vs. Bear a Tax

  • Whoever the law names — usually the seller or your employer — remits the tax (writes the check). That is not the same as bearing it, so we avoid saying who “pays.”
  • Statutory incidence = who remits. Economic incidence = who actually bears it once prices adjust. \( P_b = P_s + t \) holds no matter who remits.
  • Burden ≠ harm. The revenue is transferred to the government, not destroyed — only the DWL triangle you just saw is truly lost to everyone.

Interventions · Tax Burden

Tax Incidence & Elasticity: Same Tax, Different Damage

Identical market and identical tax \( t \) — only the elasticity of demand differs. Press → for the wedge, the burden split, then the DWL.

Inelastic demand (steep D) — buyers can’t walk away
Elastic demand (flat D) — buyers flee the price hike
It doesn’t matter who legally pays — the more inelastic side bears more of the tax, and the more elastic the market, the bigger the deadweight loss (quantity moves more, so more gains from trade vanish).

Interventions · Taxes

Why Do Taxes Generate DWL?

The Key Insight
Taxes generate deadweight loss because taxes affect relative prices — and therefore change behavior!
  • The only tax that does not change prices is a lump-sum tax (a fixed amount, independent of any choice you make).
  • Taxes affect our decisions — marriage, migration, location of investment, residential choices, having children… This is covered in depth in your ECON 101 class, and a decent proportion of my own research measures behavioral responses to tax policy changes (the elasticity of taxable income).

Interventions · Taxes

Taxes Even Affect the Timing of Death!

  • Kopczuk & Slemrod use U.S. federal estate-tax returns to ask whether the timing of death responds to its tax consequences.
  • Central estimate: for individuals dying within two weeks of a tax reform, a $10,000 potential tax saving increases the probability of dying in the lower-tax regime by 1.6%.
  • Taxes affect behavior — even the timing of behaviors we don’t usually think respond to financial incentives!

Interventions · Taxes

Taxes Even Affect Who (and When) You Marry

The U.S. taxes couples jointly, on a progressive schedule — so marriage itself moves your tax bill:

Marriage penalty
Two similar earners marry → their incomes stack into a higher bracket → combined tax rises.
Marriage bonus
One earner + a low/zero earner marry → income is effectively split into lower brackets → combined tax falls.

The tax code is not neutral to marriage — it quietly rewards some couples and penalizes others.

The behavioral response (sound familiar?)
  • Your filing status for the whole year is set by your status on Dec 31.
  • So penalized couples measurably delay a late-December wedding into January for one more year of single filing (Gelardi, 1996) — the same timing trick as the estate-tax story.

Group Work · Answers

Answers: Same Tax, Two Markets

Q1 — Bigger DWL: the elastic market (B)
Elastic demand means buyers flee the price hike, so quantity falls a lot. The DWL triangle keeps the same height \( (t) \) but gains a much wider base \( (Q^* - Q_{\text{tax}}) \) ⇒ the elastic market destroys more surplus. Inelastic demand barely moves quantity ⇒ a skinny triangle.
Q2 — Buyers bear more in the inelastic market (A)
The gap \( P_b - P^* \) is tall when demand is inelastic (buyers can’t substitute away, so they eat the tax) and short when demand is elastic (buyers walk, so sellers eat more). Whoever is less able to move pays.

Public Goods & Externalities

Why the market won’t give you clean air. Press ↓

Bridge · Back to Welfare

When Does the Market Fail Us?

Recall from Class 5
A competitive market maximizes social welfare \( (CS + PS) \). We even saw that meddling with a healthy market — with taxes or price controls — destroys welfare, opening up a deadweight loss.
So is government always the villain?
No! Sometimes the market, left alone, simply does not reach the efficient outcome. In these cases welfare is left on the table. Clean air is Exhibit A — let’s see why, and what it costs society in lost surplus.

The Air We Breathe

A Summer of Wildfire Smoke

Because no one can be stopped from breathing it, and one person’s breath leaves just as much for everyone else, clean air is a public good.

Public Goods · Property 1 of 2

Non-Rival: We All Share the Same Air

Non-rival
One person’s use does not reduce the amount available to anyone else. Your breath of clean air leaves just as much for everyone around you. Press → to let everyone breathe.

Public Goods · Property 2 of 2

Non-Excludable: You Can’t Fence the Air

Non-excludable
You cannot prevent someone from consuming it, even if they don’t pay. Suppose someone tried to sell clean air — press →.

Externalities

Wildfire Smoke: A Negative Externality

The flip side of clean air. Press →.

Externality
A cost (or benefit) landing on third parties, not in any price.
Negative externality
The polluter doesn’t pay the smoke cost ⇒ the market over-produces.

Clean air is under-provided; dirty air is over-produced. Both leave a deadweight loss.

Externalities · Back to Welfare

The Deadweight Loss of Smoke

Start from the market’s own \( CS + PS \), then add the smoke society actually pays for. Press →.

Market. \( D = MPC \Rightarrow Q_{\text{market}} \) at price \( P_{\text{mkt}} \).
Consumer surplus — buyers’ gain above the price.
Producer surplus — sellers’ gain above cost. \( CS + PS \) is the surplus the market counts.
True cost. Smoke lifts society’s cost: \( MSC = MPC + \text{ext. cost} \Rightarrow Q^* < Q_{\text{market}} \).
Subtract the damage. The red-hatched band \( (MPC \!\to\! MSC) \) is smoke society bears — take it out of \( CS + PS \).
Deadweight loss. Past \( Q^* \) the damage pokes above demand — those units cost more than they’re worth. That solid red wedge is the DWL.

Externalities · Positive

Positive Externality: Flu Shots

Start from the market’s own \( CS + PS \), then add the protection society gets for free. Press →.

Market. Buyers stop where their own benefit meets cost: \( MPB = MC \Rightarrow Q_{\text{market}} \).
Consumer surplus — buyers’ gain above the price.
Producer surplus — sellers’ gain above cost. \( CS + PS \) is the surplus the market counts.
True benefit. Your shot also protects others: \( MSB = MPB + \text{ext. benefit} \Rightarrow Q^* > Q_{\text{market}} \).
Add the bonus. The green-hatched band \( (MPB \!\to\! MSB) \) is value society gets on every shot — add it to \( CS + PS \).
Deadweight loss. Right of \( Q_{\text{market}} \), MSB still beats MC — those shots would add value but never happen. The red wedge is that forgone surplus.

Externalities · The Rule

Which Way Does the Market Miss?

An externality drives a wedge between private value (what the decision-maker feels) and social value (what everyone feels). Two cases:

Negative ⇒ over-produced
The true cost is higher than the seller pays: \( MSC > MPC \). The market trades where buyers meet \( MPC \), past the efficient point (where they’d meet \( MSC \)), so \( Q_{\text{market}} > Q^* \).

e.g. a steel mill dumping waste into a river.

Positive ⇒ under-produced
The true benefit is higher than the buyer enjoys: \( MSB > MPB \). Buyers stop where their benefit meets cost, short of where social benefit meets cost, so \( Q_{\text{market}} < Q^* \).

e.g. a flu shot that also protects classmates.

Where does the wedge sit?
A production externality moves the cost curve (\( MPC \) vs. \( MSC \)); a consumption externality moves the benefit curve (\( MPB \) vs. \( MSB \)).

Externalities · The Fix

Correcting Externalities: Tax vs. Subsidy

Make the decision-maker face the true social cost or benefit — that pushes the traded quantity to \( Q^* \):

Negative — a Pigouvian tax
Tax the seller by the external cost. Private cost rises to \( MSC \), supply shifts up/left, and the quantity falls toward \( Q^* \).

Fixes over-production (steel, pollution).

Positive — a subsidy
Subsidize the buyer by the external benefit. Effective benefit rises to \( MSB \), demand shifts up/right, and the quantity rises toward \( Q^* \).

Fixes under-production (flu shots, education).

Why It Matters

Market Failure & the Role of Government

Left to prices alone, the market misses both ways:

Public goods
Can’t be priced ⇒ under-provided.
Negative externalities
Costs land on others ⇒ over-produced.
Government steps in
Provide public goods; tax the bad, subsidize the good.
The welfare flip
Meddling with a healthy market creates DWL. Here the market is already broken, so good policy recovers welfare.

Synthesis

It All Comes Back to Welfare

One yardstick has run through Classes 5–6 — total surplus (welfare):

Setting What happens to welfare
Perfect competitionMaximized — efficient
Monopoly / market powerDeadweight loss \( (P > MC) \)
Taxes & price controlsDWL when markets are already efficient
Public goodsUnder-provided → lost surplus
Negative externalitiesOver-produced → deadweight loss
Positive externalitiesUnder-produced → lost surplus
The bottom line
Government helps when it fixes a market failure and hurts when it distorts a market that already works. Welfare is how you tell the two apart.

Quick Check

🎲 Which Way Does It Miss?

For each: positive or negative externality? Does the market make too much or too little? Which curve moves — \( MSC \) or \( MSB \)?

  • A factory dumping waste into a river
  • Your neighbour planting a beautiful garden
  • Getting a college education
  • Driving into downtown at rush hour
—
Answers
Waste = negative, \( MSC > MPC \), over-produced. Garden = positive, \( MSB > MPB \), under-produced. College = positive, \( MSB > MPB \), under-produced. Rush-hour driving = negative (congestion), \( MSC > MPC \), over-produced.

Rule of thumb: the social curve always sits above the private one — cost side for negatives, benefit side for positives.

Summary

Welfare and Efficiency

If competitive assumptions are met, market equilibrium is efficient:

  • Production costs are minimized.
  • The sum of consumer and producer surplus is maximized.
Policy Takeaway
In the absence of market failures, government interference with the competitive equilibrium via price controls or taxes leads to deadweight loss.
But when markets fail…
With public goods and externalities, the market itself is inefficient — and there, government action can raise welfare instead of lowering it.
