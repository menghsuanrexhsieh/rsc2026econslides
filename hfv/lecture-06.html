Interventions · Rationale

Why Intervene in Markets?

Market efficiency relies on several critical assumptions:

  1. Income Distribution: Competitive markets maximize total surplus given the initial distribution of income, which might be unequal or undesirable.
  2. Consumer Sovereignty: Consumers are assumed to be the best judges of their own utility.
  3. No Externalities: Producers must bear all production costs, and purchasers must receive all benefits.

If any of these assumptions fail, government intervention may be economically justified to "generate desirable market outcomes."

Interventions · Price Controls

Price Ceilings & Price Floors

Definition
Price controls are legislation that guarantees market prices — preventing prices from moving to equilibrium.
  • Price Ceiling (\( P_c \)): Legal maximum price. Binding only if **below** equilibrium (\( P_c < P^* \)), causing a shortage.
  • Price Floor (\( P_f \)): Legal minimum price. Binding only if **above** equilibrium (\( P_f > P^* \)), causing a surplus.

At any binding price control, the quantity actually transacted is always the minimum of \( Q_s \) and \( Q_d \).

Interventions · Price Floor

Policy Example: Minimum Wage

Welfare Effects of a Price Floor:

  • A minimum wage sets a legal minimum price in the labor market.
  • If set above the market equilibrium wage, it increases the quantity of labor supplied (more people want to work) but reduces the quantity of labor demanded (firms hire fewer workers).
  • This discrepancy creates a surplus of labor (unemployment) and generates a deadweight loss for society.

Interventions · Price Ceiling

Practice: Rent Control

Problem Statement
A rent control policy setting \( \$1500 \) per month rent on apartments in San Francisco is being considered. Assume apartments are identical, with demand given by: \[ P = 3600 - Q \] and supply given by: \[ P = 400 + Q \]
1. Calculate the deadweight loss due to this price ceiling.
2. If supply were perfectly inelastic at the equilibrium point, would the deadweight loss be smaller or larger?

Key concept: Shortage is the quantity mismatch (\( Q_d - Q_s \)). Deadweight loss measures the value of lost transactions.

Interventions · Solution

Solution: Rent Control

Step 1: Find Market Equilibrium (No Intervention)
\[ 3600 - Q = 400 + Q \implies 3200 = 2Q \implies Q^* = 1600, P^* = \$2000 \]
Step 2: Apply Price Ceiling (\( P_c = 1500 \))
At \( P = 1500 \), supply is binding: \( Q_S = 1500 - 400 = 1100 \) units (Shortage: \( Q_D - Q_S = 2100 - 1100 = 1000 \)).
Step 3: Calculate Deadweight Loss (DWL)
At \( Q = 1100 \), demand valuation is \( P_D = 3600 - 1100 = 2500 \); MC is \( P_S = 1500 \). \[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times (Q^* - Q_S) \times (P_D - P_S) = \frac{1}{2} \times (1600 - 1100) \times (2500 - 1500) \] \[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times 500 \times 1000 = \$250,000 \]
Part 2: Inelastic Supply
If supply is perfectly inelastic at \( Q = 1600 \), quantity remains unchanged at 1600. No output is lost, so DWL is zero (which is smaller).

Interventions · Taxes

Prices Controlled by Taxes

How do taxes affect market outcomes?

  • Governments artificially change transaction prices by introducing per-unit or percentage taxes.
  • A tax creates a wedge between what consumers pay (\( P_b \)) and what sellers receive (\( P_s \)).
  • This tax wedge restricts the quantity traded and creates deadweight loss.
Tax Identity
\[ P_b = P_s + t \] where \( t \) is the per-unit tax rate.

Interventions · Tax Example

Practice: Producer Tax & Welfare

Problem Statement
Suppose supply and demand for a competitively supplied product are given as: \[ Q_D = 150 - 25P \quad\text{or}\quad P = 6 - 0.04Q_D \] \[ Q_S = 50P \quad\text{or}\quad P = 0.02Q_S \]
1. Find the equilibrium market price and quantity.
2. The government imposes a per-unit tax of $0.30 on suppliers. Calculate:
  • New equilibrium price paid by consumers (\( P_b \)).
  • Price received by sellers (\( P_s \)).
  • Quantity transacted (\( Q_{\text{tax}} \)).
  • The economic tax incidence on buyers and sellers.
3. Calculate the deadweight loss (DWL) resulting from this tax.

Interventions · Solution

Solution: Producer Tax & Welfare

Step 1: Equilibrium without Tax
\[ 150 - 25P = 50P \implies 75P = 150 \implies P^* = \$2.00, Q^* = 100 \text{ units} \]
Step 2: Solve with Tax wedge (\( P_b = P_s + 0.30 \))
Substitute buyers' price into demand: \[ 150 - 25(P_s + 0.30) = 50P_s \implies 150 - 25P_s - 7.5 = 50P_s \] \[ 142.5 = 75P_s \implies P_s = \$1.90 \implies P_b = 1.90 + 0.30 = \$2.20 \] Quantity transacted: \( Q_{\text{tax}} = 50(1.90) = 95 \) units.
Step 3: Tax Incidence (Who pays the tax?)
Price rise for consumers: \( \Delta P_b = P_b - P^* = 2.20 - 2.00 = \$0.20 \). (Consumers pay \( 2/3 \) of the tax).
Price drop for sellers: \( \Delta P_s = P^* - P_s = 2.00 - 1.90 = \$0.10 \). (Sellers pay \( 1/3 \) of the tax).
Economic incidence depends on elasticities (the more inelastic side bears more burden). Here, demand is relatively more inelastic.
Step 4: Calculate Deadweight Loss (DWL)
Taxes prevent mutually beneficial trades (units 96 to 100). The loss in social surplus is: \[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times \Delta Q \times t = \frac{1}{2} \times (100 - 95) \times \$0.30 = \$0.75 \]

Interventions · Tax Burden

Tax Incidence & Elasticities

Key Principle
It does not matter on whom the tax is legally imposed; the economic burden (incidence) depends entirely on the relative elasticities of supply and demand.

Economic Incidence Formulas:

  • Incidence on Buyers: \( \text{Burden}_C = \frac{\varepsilon_S}{\varepsilon_S + |\varepsilon_D|} \)
  • Incidence on Sellers: \( \text{Burden}_P = \frac{|\varepsilon_D|}{\varepsilon_S + |\varepsilon_D|} \)

The side of the market that is more inelastic (less responsive to price changes) bears the larger share of the tax burden.

Interventions · Taxes

Why Do Taxes Generate DWL?

The Key Insight
Taxes generate deadweight loss because taxes affect relative prices — and therefore change behavior!
  • The only tax that does not change prices is a lump-sum tax (a fixed amount, independent of any choice you make).
  • Side note: taxes affect our decisions! E.g. marriage, migration, location of investment, residential choices, having children…
  • This is covered in depth in your ECON 101 class — and a decent proportion of Rex’s own research measures behavioral responses to tax policy changes (the elasticity of taxable income).

Interventions · Taxes

Taxes Even Affect the Timing of Death!

  • Kopczuk & Slemrod use U.S. federal estate-tax returns to ask whether the timing of death responds to its tax consequences.
  • Central estimate: for individuals dying within two weeks of a tax reform, a $10,000 potential tax saving increases the probability of dying in the lower-tax regime by 1.6%.
  • Taxes affect behavior — even the timing of behaviors we don’t usually think respond to financial incentives!

The Air We Breathe

A Summer of Wildfire Smoke

Bridge · Back to Welfare

When Does the Market Fail Us?

Recall from Class 5
A competitive market maximizes social welfare \( (CS + PS) \). We even saw that meddling with a healthy market — with taxes or price controls — destroys welfare, opening up a deadweight loss.
So is government always the villain?
No — that result quietly assumed there were no market failures. Sometimes the market, left alone, simply does not reach the efficient outcome.
Public goods & externalities are those failures
In these cases welfare is left on the table. Clean air is Exhibit A — let’s see why, and what it costs society in lost surplus.

Definition

What Makes a Good a Public Good?

A public good has two defining properties:

1 · Non-excludable
You cannot prevent someone from consuming it, even if they don’t pay. No one can be stopped from breathing the air around them.
2 · Non-rival
One person’s use does not reduce the amount available to anyone else. Your breath of clean air leaves just as much for everyone around you.
Clean air ticks both boxes
Clean air is the textbook public good — non-excludable and non-rival. So are national defense, a lighthouse’s beam, and radio broadcasts.

Externalities

Wildfire Smoke: A Negative Externality

The flip side of clean air. Press →.

Externality
A cost (or benefit) imposed on third parties that is not reflected in any market price.
Negative externality
Smoke, pollution, and congestion pile costs on others. Because the polluter doesn’t pay them, the market over-produces the harm — the traded quantity runs past the socially efficient one, and welfare is lost.

Two sides of one coin: clean air (a public good) is under-provided; dirty air (a negative externality) is over-produced. Prices capture neither — and both leave a deadweight loss.

Externalities · Back to Welfare

The Deadweight Loss of Smoke

The same deadweight-loss triangle from Class 5 — but now the market itself draws it. Press →.

The market trades where buyers meet private cost \( (D = MPC) \), at \( Q_{\text{market}} \).
With smoke, social cost is higher: \( MSC = MPC + \text{external cost} \). Society should stop at \( Q^* < Q_{\text{market}} \), so the market over-produces — every extra unit costs more than it’s worth.
The red triangle is the deadweight loss — the very same welfare tool from Class 5, now drawn by the free market itself.

Externalities · The Rule

Which Way Does the Market Miss?

An externality drives a wedge between private value (what the decision-maker feels) and social value (what everyone feels). Two cases:

Negative ⇒ over-produced
The true cost is higher than the seller pays: \( MSC > MPC \). The market trades where buyers meet \( MPC \), past the efficient point (where they’d meet \( MSC \)), so \( Q_{\text{market}} > Q^* \).

e.g. a steel mill dumping waste into a river.

Positive ⇒ under-produced
The true benefit is higher than the buyer enjoys: \( MSB > MPB \). Buyers stop where their benefit meets cost, short of where social benefit meets cost, so \( Q_{\text{market}} < Q^* \).

e.g. a flu shot that also protects classmates.

Where does the wedge sit?
A production externality moves the cost curve (\( MPC \) vs. \( MSC \)); a consumption externality moves the benefit curve (\( MPB \) vs. \( MSB \)).

Externalities · Positive

Positive Externality: Flu Shots

A flu shot protects the buyer and the people around them — a benefit the buyer never counts. Press →.

Buyers get shots where their own benefit meets the cost \( (MPB = S) \), at \( Q_{\text{market}} \).
But each shot also protects others: \( MSB = MPB + \text{external benefit} \). Society should reach \( Q^* > Q_{\text{market}} \) — the market makes too few.
The red triangle is the deadweight loss from under-provision — surplus left on the table.

Externalities · The Fix

Correcting Externalities: Tax vs. Subsidy

Make the decision-maker face the true social cost or benefit — that pushes the traded quantity to \( Q^* \):

Negative — a Pigouvian tax
Tax the seller by the external cost. Private cost rises to \( MSC \), supply shifts up/left, and the quantity falls toward \( Q^* \).

Fixes over-production (steel, pollution).

Positive — a subsidy
Subsidize the buyer by the external benefit. Effective benefit rises to \( MSB \), demand shifts up/right, and the quantity rises toward \( Q^* \).

Fixes under-production (flu shots, education).

One idea
A tax shrinks a market that harms others; a subsidy grows a market that helps others. Both move the traded quantity to the socially efficient level and erase the deadweight loss.

Why It Matters

Market Failure & the Role of Government

Left to prices alone, the market gets both cases wrong:

Public goods
Can’t be priced ⇒ no incentive to produce ⇒ under-provided (too little clean air, defense, research).
Negative externalities
Costs fall on others, not the price ⇒ over-produced (too much smoke, pollution).
This is where government steps in
Provide public goods directly, funded by taxes (recall Part 1); tax or regulate negative externalities (emissions limits, wildfire management) and subsidize positive ones (vaccines, education) so prices finally reflect the true social cost and benefit.
The welfare flip
In Class 5, intervening in a healthy market created deadweight loss. Here the free-market outcome is already inefficient, so well-aimed intervention recovers lost welfare. Same yardstick — opposite verdict.

Synthesis

It All Comes Back to Welfare

One yardstick has run through Classes 5–6 — total surplus (welfare):

Setting What happens to welfare
Perfect competitionMaximized — efficient
Monopoly / market powerDeadweight loss \( (P > MC) \)
Taxes & price controlsDeadweight loss
Public goodsUnder-provided → lost surplus
Negative externalitiesOver-produced → deadweight loss
Positive externalitiesUnder-produced → lost surplus
The bottom line
Government helps when it fixes a market failure and hurts when it distorts a market that already works. Welfare is how you tell the two apart.

Summary

Welfare and Efficiency

If competitive assumptions are met, market equilibrium is efficient:

  • Production costs are minimized.
  • The sum of consumer and producer surplus is maximized.
Policy Takeaway
In the absence of market failures, government interference with the competitive equilibrium via price controls or taxes leads to deadweight loss.
But when markets fail…
With public goods and externalities, the market itself is inefficient — and there, government action can raise welfare instead of lowering it.
