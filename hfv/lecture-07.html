Finitely Repeated · 1/4

The Fun Begins When There's an End in Sight

Suppose Philips and Sony play the same entry game — but only twice.

Sony
Enter NowStay Out
PhilipsEnter Now30, 3014, 55
Stay Out55, 1420, 20

There's now an end in sight — namely, the second round. The NE (in pure strategies) of the stage game is still (Stay Out, Stay Out).

What must happen in the second (final) round?

Finitely Repeated · 2/4

Iterating the Logic

  • In the final round, there is no future to protect — both players simply play the stage-game NE: (Stay Out, Stay Out).
  • But then, in the second-to-last round, cooperation cannot be rewarded (the last round is already lost) — so both defect there too.
  • Iterate: do the players have incentives to deviate from the NE in the 5th round? The 10th round? No — the whole game unravels from the end!

Finitely Repeated · 3/4

Why Did We Reach This Conclusion?

The Endgame Problem
If there is a definite end to the game, both players know there is no benefit to cooperating in the last round — so they defect at the end, and (by iteration) all the way back to round one.

We say finite games suffer from non-credible threats: the threat of “pulling the grim trigger” has no bite when the game is finite.

By extension, threats and promises are also not credible in the second-to-last round, third-to-last round, etc.

Finitely Repeated · 4/4

How to Solve Finite Games, Then?

Finite games have an endgame. So let's start from the end, and work backwards!

Definition: Backward Induction
Backward induction is the way to solve finitely repeated (and sequential) games: start at the end of the game and work backwards in “time”, to figure out the equilibrium strategies and payoffs.

Let's see how backward induction works in sequential move games!

Sequential Games · Definition

Sequential Move Games

Definition: Sequential Move Game
A sequential move game is a game where players take turns deciding what moves to play.

Let's see how the equilibrium of a given game changes — if at all — if it were played in:

  1. simultaneous move, one-shot;
  2. simultaneous move, finitely repeated;
  3. simultaneous move, infinitely repeated;
  4. sequential moves.

Sequential Games · Running Example

Offering a Warranty Becomes a Game

Long Example
Honda and Suzuki are competing motorcycle manufacturers. Both are simultaneously considering offering 10-year warranties — costly, but making their vehicles more attractive relative to the competitor's.
Suzuki
OfferDon't Offer
HondaOffer25, 3585, 15
Don't Offer20, 9550, 55

Question 0. If the game is played once, what is the NE?

Sequential Games · Solution

Solution: One-Shot NE

Best Responses
Honda: vs. Offer, 25 > 20; vs. Don't Offer, 85 > 50 ⇒ Offer is dominant.
Suzuki: vs. Offer, 35 > 15; vs. Don't Offer, 95 > 55 ⇒ Offer is dominant.
Conclusion
The unique NE (in dominant strategies) is (Offer, Offer) = (25, 35) — even though both would prefer (Don't, Don't) = (50, 55). A classic prisoner's dilemma!

Sequential Games · Practice

Practice: Repeat It 23 Times

Question 1
Now, suppose the game is repeated 23 times. What is the outcome of the game? Is it the same as what you described in Question 0? Why or why not?

(Hint: use backward induction — no maths is needed to answer this question.)

Sequential Games · Solution

Solution: 23 Repetitions

Backward Induction
In round 23 (the last round) there is no future — both play the dominant strategy: (Offer, Offer). But then nothing can be rewarded in round 22 either… and the game unravels all the way to round 1.
Conclusion
(Offer, Offer) in every round — exactly the same as Question 0. The endgame problem strikes: with a finite horizon, cooperation cannot be sustained.

Sequential Games · Practice

Practice: Now Repeat It Forever

Question 2
Now the game is infinitely repeated. Honda and Suzuki agreed, contractually, to not offer warranties. Both companies want to use a grim trigger strategy to deter the other from defecting from the agreement.

At what level of interest rate / discount factor \( r \) does the grim trigger constitute a NE in this infinitely repeated game?
Suzuki
OfferDon't Offer
HondaOffer25, 3585, 15
Don't Offer20, 9550, 55

Sequential Games · Solution

Solution: When Does the Grim Trigger Work?

Honda: Cooperate vs. Defect
Cooperate (Don't, Don't) forever: \( 50 + \frac{50}{r} \). Defect once (grab 85), then punished at (Offer, Offer) forever: \( 85 + \frac{25}{r} \). \[ 50 + \frac{50}{r} \ge 85 + \frac{25}{r} \iff \frac{25}{r} \ge 35 \iff r \le \frac{5}{7} \approx 0.71 \]
Suzuki: Cooperate vs. Defect
Cooperate: \( 55 + \frac{55}{r} \). Defect once (grab 95), then \( 35 \) forever: \( 95 + \frac{35}{r} \). \[ 55 + \frac{55}{r} \ge 95 + \frac{35}{r} \iff \frac{20}{r} \ge 40 \iff r \le \frac{1}{2} \]
Conclusion
Both conditions must hold ⇒ the grim trigger is a NE iff \( r \le 0.5 \) (Suzuki's condition binds — Suzuki is more tempted to defect).

Sequential Games · Extensive Form

Q3: The Move Structure Changes

Question 3
Honda decides in January 2019 whether to offer the warranty. Suzuki decides in March 2019. The payoffs are the same. Analyze the outcome via backward induction.
Definition: Extensive Form & Game Tree
The extensive form of a sequential game shows actions taken by players in succession, in a game tree. The order in which players move matters — it affects how we draw the tree! Payoffs sit at the ends of the tree (the leaves).

Sequential Games · Game Tree

The Warranty Game, in Extensive Form

Honda moves first — it is the parent (root) of the game tree. Suzuki moves second: conditional on what Honda does, we transfer the payoffs from the normal form to the leaves.

Sequential Games · Backward Induction

Solving the Tree, Backwards

Step 1: Suzuki moves last — in each branch it picks its best reply. Step 2: Honda, anticipating Suzuki, compares 25 (Offer) vs. 20 (Not Offer) ⇒ Offer.

Sequential Games · SPE

Subgame Perfect Equilibrium

Definition: Subgame Perfect Equilibrium (SPE)
A subgame perfect equilibrium is a Nash equilibrium in every subgame of the game.
  • Think of each “branch” as a subgame: one where Honda plays Offer, another where Honda plays Not Offer.
  • The backward-induction answer to Q3 is the SPE: Honda plays Offer; Suzuki plays (Offer if Offer; Offer if Not Offer). Outcome: (25, 35).

Sequential Games · Order of Moves

Q4: What If Suzuki Moves First?

Question 4
Now Suzuki decides in January 2019, and Honda decides in March 2019. Does this change the outcome of our analysis? Why / why not?
Answer
No. Offer is a dominant strategy for both players — each plays Offer no matter what the other does, so the move order doesn't matter here. Outcome: (Offer, Offer) = (25, 35), again.

But in general, the order can matter — when the game has no dominant-strategy equilibrium!

Sequential Games · First-Mover Advantage

Who Moves First Can Change Everything

Two coworkers each prefer to avoid doing what the other does (payoffs: same choice ⇒ 0, 0). There is no DSE — and the SPE depends on who moves first: the first mover steers the game to their preferred equilibrium.

Sequential Games · Takeaways

Takeaways From Sequential Move Games

  1. We represent sequential move games in extensive form, using game trees.
  2. Backward induction solves finitely repeated and sequential games.
  3. The order of moves matters for the SPE when the game has no DSE — there is usually first-mover advantage in such games.
  4. Move structures can dramatically change our equilibrium predictions, even if payoffs don't change.
  5. Mark all the off-equilibrium paths: rational players never go down them, but they are part of the beliefs we need to state the SPE. In a SPE, players best-respond both on and off the equilibrium path!

Group Work · Answers

Credible Threats · Incumbent–Entrant

Honda Wants to Enter a New Space…

Suzuki already has significant market power in distributing certain motorcycle parts. Honda wants to enter this space. We model this as an incumbent–entrant game.

Question 0. Use backward induction to solve this game. What is the SPE?

Credible Threats · Solution

Solution: The SPE

Suzuki (moving last): 50 > 30 ⇒ Accommodate. Honda, anticipating this: 30 > 0 ⇒ Enter. SPE outcome: (30, 50).

Credible Threats · The Incumbent's Problem

Q1: Suzuki's Problem

Question 1
Given your answer to Question 0, what is the problem here for Suzuki (the incumbent)? Can it do better than the SPE you found? Describe in words.
Answer
Suzuki would love Honda to Stay Out (payoff 100 > 50). It could threaten to Fight… but once Honda has entered, Fighting pays 30 < 50 — a rational Suzuki would Accommodate. The threat is non-credible, and Honda knows it!
Definition: Credible Threat
A threat is credible if, when the time comes, carrying it out is in the threatener's own best interest.

Credible Threats · Commitment

Q2: Suzuki Brings a Gun to a Knife-Fight

Question 2 — Turn “Fight” into a credible threat
Suzuki anticipates Honda's potential entry, and considers preemptively expanding its production capacity. The expansion costs $30,000, which is wasted unless a price war happens — i.e. the cost is borne if Honda plays “Stay Out” or Suzuki plays “Accommodate”. Assume the “Fight” payoff already incorporates the $30,000.

New payoffs: Fight (−10, 30); Accommodate (30, 20); Stay Out (0, 70).

Does Honda enter? What are the profits in equilibrium?

Credible Threats · Solution

Solution: Fight Becomes Credible

With the capacity in place, Suzuki now prefers to Fight after entry (30 > 20). Honda anticipates this: entering yields −10 < 0 ⇒ Stay Out. SPE outcome: (0, 70) — entry deterred!

Credible Threats · The Full Game

Q3: Completing the Game Tree

Question 3
The tree in Questions 0–1 is the game without preemptive capacity investment. The tree in Question 2 is the game with it. Draw the full game tree encompassing both scenarios.

Given your full tree: what will Suzuki (the incumbent) do before Honda decides? Will Suzuki make the preemptive investment?

Credible Threats · Solution

Solution: The Full Game Tree

Suzuki moves first: Don't invest ⇒ branch worth 50 (Honda enters, Suzuki accommodates). Invest ⇒ branch worth 70 (Fight credible, Honda stays out). Since 70 > 50, Suzuki invests: commitment pays!

Credible Threats · Takeaway

The Value of Commitment

  • An empty threat changes nothing — rational rivals see through it.
  • By sinking a cost up front, Suzuki changed its own future incentives — making the threat to Fight genuinely optimal to carry out.
  • Paradox of commitment: burning money (or bridges!) can make you better off, because it changes how others expect you to behave.

Real-world examples: capacity expansion, exclusive contracts, loyalty pricing, “we will match any price” guarantees…

Summary & Wrap-Up

Summary

What We Learned Today

  • Finite games unravel: the endgame problem kills cooperation, because grim-trigger threats are non-credible with an end in sight.
  • Backward induction: solve sequential games from the last move backwards; the result is a subgame perfect equilibrium — a NE in every subgame.
  • Order matters (without a DSE): expect first-mover advantage.
  • Credible threats: a threat only works if carrying it out is in your own interest — and commitment (sinking costs) can make it so.
