Class 3 Recap · Marginal Revenue

Marginal Revenue & Point Elasticity

Marginal revenue (MR) is the extra revenue from selling one more unit. To sell more, a firm must lower the price on every unit — so MR falls below the price:

\[ MR(Q) = \frac{d\big(P(Q)\,Q\big)}{dQ} = P(Q)\left(1 + \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) \]
  • Because demand slopes down, \( \varepsilon < 0 \), so \( \left(1 + \tfrac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) < 1 \) and \( MR < P \) always.
  • Elastic demand (\( |\varepsilon| > 1 \)) \( \Rightarrow MR > 0 \): selling more still raises revenue.
  • Inelastic demand (\( |\varepsilon| < 1 \)) \( \Rightarrow MR < 0 \): selling more lowers revenue.

Class 3 Recap · Marginal Revenue

Marginal Revenue from Linear Demand

Linear inverse demand
\[ P = a - bQ \] \[ R(Q) = (a - bQ)Q = aQ - bQ^2 \] \[ MR(Q) = a - 2bQ \]
Rule of thumb
Same intercept \( a \), but twice the slope — so MR hits the quantity axis at half the demand curve's distance.

Class 3 Recap · Monopoly Pricing

Solving for the Monopolist's Price

A monopolist faces the whole market demand and chooses quantity first, then price, using the rule \( MR = MC \):

  1. Set up: from demand, get inverse demand \( P = a - bQ \) and \( MR = a - 2bQ \).
  2. Quantity: set \( MR = MC \) and solve for \( Q^* \).
  3. Price: read it off the demand curve, \( P^* = P(Q^*) = a - bQ^* \).
  4. Profit: \( \Pi = P^* Q^* - TC(Q^*) \).
Key intuition
The monopolist does not pick an arbitrary price — it is pinned down by the demand curve at \( Q^* \).

Perfect Competition · Intro

Introducing Competitors

How do a firm's decisions change when other firms enter the market?

To analyze the impact of competition, we must consider two dimensions:

  1. Market Structure: How many competitors are there? Do they sell identical or differentiated products?
  2. Market Equilibrium Implications: How does the number of competitors affect prices, quantity, and long-term economic profits?

Let's start by looking at the competitive extreme: Perfect Competition.

Perfect Competition · Structure

Market Characteristics

Key features of a perfectly competitive environment:

  • Large number of sellers and buyers; no individual has market power.
  • Homogeneous products: Only identical products are sold in the market, with no product differentiation.
  • No barriers to entry or exit: firms can freely enter when profits are positive and exit when they are negative.
  • Perfect information: Buyers and sellers have complete knowledge of prices and technology.
⇒ Everyone is a Price Taker
Together these imply no single buyer or seller can influence the market price — the (aggregate) market demand and supply decide the price, and every firm simply takes it as given!

Perfect Competition · Definition

Defining Perfect Competition

Definition: Perfect Competition
A form of competitive environment characterized by a large number of sellers and buyers, with a homogeneous, undifferentiated product, where every firm and every individual takes market prices as a given.

Real-world examples:

  • Markets of produce (corn, rice, grains, wheat).
  • Markets of standardized knockoffs/commodities.

Market Demand vs. Firm Demand

Question to consider:

Individual Demand is Perfectly Elastic
A single firm is too small to affect the market. It can sell any quantity it wants at the market price \( P \), but nothing at a higher price.
Firm Demand is flat (horizontal) at \( P \implies |\varepsilon| = \infty \)

Perfect Competition · Summary

Characteristics Recap

Therefore, perfectly competitive markets are characterized by:

  • Large numbers of sellers and buyers + only identical products sold in the market, with no product differentiation.
  • Market prices are taken as given by everyone.
  • Demand each firm faces is perfectly elastic.
  • Each firm’s supply is determined by its marginal costs (with some additional twists!).

Market Equilibrium · Introduction

Implications for Equilibrium

How do price-taking firms behave in equilibrium in a perfectly competitive market?

  • So far, we have only described the environment, not the equilibrium.
  • The fact that every firm takes prices as given — rather than setting prices themselves — must have consequences for the market equilibrium!

Market Equilibrium · Supply

Is Marginal Cost the Supply Curve?

Firms choose quantity where \( MR = MC \). Since \( MR = P \), this means competitive firms choose quantity where:

P = MC(Q)

Does the MC curve fully represent the firm's supply decision?

  • Yes, but with nuances: The firm may choose to produce zero (shut down) if the price is too low to cover certain costs.
  • The distinction depends on whether we look at the short run or the long run.

Market Equilibrium · Profits

Market Equilibrium and Profits

Market equilibrium is a statement about at what price and quantity the market demand “equals” market supply.

  • Every firm takes the market price as given, so this definition has no “bite” for whether a firm would make positive economic profits.
  • To determine profits, we need to look at the firm’s cost functions:
\[ \text{Profit} = (P^* - ATC(Q^*)) \times Q^* \]

Short-Run Equilibrium · Definition

Defining Short-Run Equilibrium

Definition: Short-Run Equilibrium
The state where firms choose production levels considering both fixed and variable costs, with the number of firms in the industry fixed (no entry/exit).

In the short run, capital is fixed, meaning firms face fixed costs and cannot immediately exit the industry or enter it.

Short-Run Equilibrium · Supply

The Short-Run Supply Curve

The Shutdown Rule:

  • The short-run supply curve is the section of the \( MC \) curve above the \( AVC \) minimum.
  • If \( P < AVC \), the firm produces zero (\( Q = 0 \)) and shuts down.
Takeaway: In short-run market equilibrium, firms will still operate even if they make operating loss, provided they cover variable costs. If they cannot cover variable costs, they shut down.

Short-Run Equilibrium · Dynamics

Free Entry and Exit

Market Adjustments:

  • Short run is also characterized by free entry and exit due to no barriers to entry.
  • Every firm is selling identical, undifferentiated products, so barriers to entry are low.

Why might new firms enter, and existing firms exit, if everyone takes the market price as given?

Short-Run Equilibrium · Entry/Exit

Illustrating Entry Dynamics

In the short run:

  • Possible for firms in competitive markets to earn economic profit.
  • Profit encourages new firms to enter the market.
  • Market supply shifts to the right.
  • If profits are negative, the same story applies, but in reverse.

Long-Run Equilibrium · Definition

Defining Long-Run Equilibrium

Definition: Long-Run Equilibrium
Long-run equilibrium is where firms consider only variable costs in making their production decisions.

Practical consequences:

  • Firms view fixed costs as sunk, hence the only thing that characterizes any long-run equilibrium is zero profits.
  • Why? Because if profits are not zero, there will continue to be free entry and exit, and that is a function of fixed costs, too.

Long-Run Equilibrium · Price

The Long-Run Equilibrium Price

In long-run equilibrium, free entry drives the price down to \( P^* = \min(ATC) \), where every firm earns exactly zero economic profit. (Market on the right; representative firm on the left.)

Short-Run vs. Long-Run Equilibria

Short-Run Equilibrium
Economic profits can be positive or negative or zero, there is free entry and exit, and shutdown occurs when the market price cannot cover variable costs.
Long-Run Equilibrium
All firms earn zero economic profits, due to free exit and entry going from short-run to long-run.

Perfect Competition · Example 1

Fred's Hats Calculation

Problem Statement
Fred’s Hats, a company involved in the production of University of Michigan Rose Bowl hats, has total cost and marginal cost functions as follows: \[ TC(q) = q^2 + 2q + 49 \] \[ MC(q) = 2q + 2 \] where \( q \) is the quantity of hats produced by Fred’s Hats and marginal cost is measured in dollars per unit. Fred’s Hats operates in a perfectly competitive market, where the market price for these hats is currently $20 each.

Calculate the short-run profit maximizing quantity of hats that Fred’s Hats should produce, and the profit that they make. Then, state whether the market is in a long-run equilibrium.

Perfect Competition · Solution 1

Solution: Fred's Hats

Step 1: Profit-Maximizing Quantity
In perfect competition, \( P = MR = MC \): \[ 20 = 2q + 2 \implies 18 = 2q \implies q^* = 9 \]
Step 2: Profit at \( q^* = 9 \)
\[ TR = 20 \cdot 9 = 180 \qquad TC = 81 + 18 + 49 = 148 \] \[ \Pi = TR - TC = 180 - 148 = 32 \]
Step 3: Long-Run Equilibrium?
\[ ATC(q^*) = \frac{148}{9} \approx 16.44 \] Since \( P = 20 > ATC = 16.44 \), profit is positive.

Not in long-run equilibrium → entry will drive price down.

Perfect Competition · Example 2

Fred's Hats: Long-Run Equilibrium

Problem Statement (continued)
Suppose market demand for these hats is given by: \[ Q_D = 220 - 5P_D \quad \text{or} \quad P_D = 44 - 0.2Q_D \] and the cost structure for Fred’s Hats remains the same as those on the previous slide.

Calculate the long-run equilibrium market price.

Hint: In the long run, profits are zero, which means price must equal the minimum of ATC.

Perfect Competition · Solution 2

Solution: Long-Run Equilibrium

Key Principle
In long-run equilibrium, free entry drives profits to zero: \[ P^* = \min(ATC) \]
Step 1: Find min(ATC) via ATC = MC
\[ ATC = q + 2 + \frac{49}{q} \qquad MC = 2q + 2 \] \[ ATC = MC \implies \frac{49}{q} = q \implies q^2 = 49 \implies q = 7 \]
Step 2: Long-Run Equilibrium Price
\[ P^* = \min(ATC) = 7 + 2 + \frac{49}{7} = 7 + 2 + 7 = 16 \]

Long-run equilibrium price = $16

Group Work · Answers

Answers: Price Takers

Question 1: P = MC
Price takers produce where price equals marginal cost: \[ 10 = 2Q + 2 \implies Q^* = 4 \]
Question 2: Profit & the Long Run
\[ ATC(4) = \frac{8}{4} + 2 + 4 = 8 \qquad \Pi = (P - ATC) \times Q = (10 - 8) \times 4 = \$8 > 0 \]

Positive economic profit attracts entry: market supply shifts out and the price falls toward minimum ATC, until long-run profits are zero. Let’s see it graphically ↓
