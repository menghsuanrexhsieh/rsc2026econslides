Recap · 1 of 4

(Own-Price) Elasticity of Demand

Definition
The elasticity of demand is the ratio of percentage change in quantity to the percentage change in price (also called point elasticity of demand).

We define the own-price elasticity of demand \( \varepsilon \) as:

\[ \varepsilon = \frac{\% \Delta Q}{\% \Delta P} \]
\[ \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta Q / Q}{\Delta P / P} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q} \]

Where \( \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \) is the slope of the demand function.
By definition, \( \varepsilon \) is negative, but we follow tradition and work with the absolute value \( |\varepsilon| \).

Recap · 2 of 4

Relatively Elastic vs. Inelastic

Recap · 3 of 4

Elasticity regions with linear demand

Elasticity regions on a linear curve:

  • Elastic above the midpoint: \( |\varepsilon| > 1 \)
  • Unit-elastic at the midpoint: \( |\varepsilon| = 1 \)
  • Inelastic below the midpoint: \( |\varepsilon| < 1 \)

Recap · 4 of 4

Income & Cross-Price Elasticities

Definition: Cross-Price Elasticity
For any two goods \( X \) and \( Y \), if the cross-price elasticity of demand for \( X \) with respect to the price of \( Y \) is:
  • Negative: then \( X \) and \( Y \) are complements.
  • Positive: then \( X \) and \( Y \) are substitutes.
Definition: Income Elasticity
For any good \( X \), if the income elasticity of demand for \( X \) is:
  • Negative: then \( X \) is an inferior good.
  • Positive: then \( X \) is a normal good.

Note: We must infer elasticities from context in the real world!

Elasticity & Marginal Revenue

The Unit-Elastic Midpoint Rule

Key Fact
For a firm facing a given demand function, the firm’s marginal revenue is zero when they set a price where the customer’s demand is unit-elastic (\( |\varepsilon| = 1 \)).

This represents the peak of total revenue (midpoint for linear demand). This explains why firms care about knowing elasticity!

Elasticity & Marginal Revenue

What is Marginal Revenue?

Definition
Marginal revenue (MR) is the incremental revenue made by firms when they produce one additional unit of product.

This is the concept of marginal benefits for firms! Mathematically, we derive it from the total revenue function:

\[ MR = \frac{d(TR)}{dQ} \]

Now we discuss firms and introduce formal connections to elasticity!

Profit Maximization · Setup

Firms in the Supply/Demand Model

Three basic facts (assumptions) in our model:

Fact #1. Firms’ objective is to maximize profits = total revenue minus total costs.
Fact #2. Firms choose quantity to maximize profits.
Fact #3 (!). Firms’ demand (they face) may differ from the market demand for a product they sell.

Profit Maximization · Setup

The Profit Function

Combining Fact #1 & #2, we define the profit function where the firm chooses quantity:

\[ \Pi(Q) = R(Q) - C(Q) \]

Where \( R(Q) \) is the revenue function and \( C(Q) \) is the cost function.

Profit Maximization · Formulation

Inverse Demand Function

Since we are analyzing firms choosing quantity, we cannot work directly with standard demand functions anymore (which express quantity in terms of price).

Definition
An inverse demand function expresses price as a function of quantity. A linear inverse demand takes the form: \[ P = a - bQ \]

This allows us to write the price at which a given quantity can be sold.

Profit Maximization · Formulation

The Revenue Function

The revenue function, expressed in terms of firms choosing quantity to produce, is defined as:

\[ R(Q) = P(Q) \times Q \]

For a linear inverse demand curve \( P = a - bQ \), the revenue function is:

\[ R(Q) = (a - bQ)Q = aQ - bQ^2 \]

We will come back to costs, written as \( C(Q) \), later today.

Profit Maximization · Formulation

Deriving Marginal Revenue

Marginal revenue is the derivative of the revenue function with respect to quantity. For a linear inverse demand:

\[ R(Q) = aQ - bQ^2 \] Applying the power rule, we get the marginal revenue function: \[ MR(Q) = a - 2bQ \]
Key Relationship
The marginal revenue function shares the same intercept (\( a \)) as the linear inverse demand function, but has twice the slope (\( -2b \))!

Profit Maximization · Derivation

Relating MR to Elasticity of Demand

Using the definition of demand elasticity \( \varepsilon \) and inverse demand slope:

\[ \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q} \implies \frac{\Delta P}{\Delta Q} = \frac{P}{Q} \frac{1}{\varepsilon} \]

Deriving Marginal Revenue using the product rule:

\[ MR(Q) = \frac{d(P(Q) \times Q)}{dQ} = P'(Q) \times Q + P(Q) \] \[ MR(Q) = \left( \frac{P(Q)}{Q} \frac{1}{\varepsilon} \right) Q + P(Q) = P(Q)\left(1 + \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) \]

This is the general relation linking price, marginal revenue, and elasticity!

Profit Maximization · Intuition

Intuition behind the MR Equation

\[ MR(Q) = P(Q)\left(1 + \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) \]
  • Notice that \( P(Q) \) is the price at quantity \( Q \).
  • Because elasticity \( \varepsilon \) is negative, the term \( \left(1 + \frac{1}{\varepsilon}\right) \) is less than 1.
  • The Takeaway: When consumers are sensitive to price changes (elastic demand), firms have the incentive to lower prices to earn positive marginal revenue!

Profit Maximization · Graphing

Plotting MR alongside Demand

For any linear inverse demand curve \( P = a - bQ \), we plot the corresponding marginal revenue curve \( MR(Q) = a - 2bQ \) directly below it:

Visualizing the curves
Both start at the same vertical intercept (\( a \)), but the MR curve intercepts the horizontal (quantity) axis at exactly half the distance of the demand curve.

Profit Maximization · Example

Calculation Exercise

Problem Statement
Given the demand function: \[ Q = 10 - 0.5P \]
  1. Calculate the inverse demand function and marginal revenue function.
  2. Plot the marginal revenue function alongside the inverse demand function.
  3. Calculate the marginal revenue and elasticity of demand when \( P = 10 \).

Profit Max · Solution

Solution: Calculation Exercise

1. Inverse Demand & MR
\[ Q = 10 - 0.5P \implies P = 20 - 2Q \] \[ R(Q) = (20 - 2Q)Q = 20Q - 2Q^2 \] \[ MR(Q) = 20 - 4Q \]
2. Plot MR alongside Demand

Same vertical intercept (20), but MR has twice the slope (−4 vs. −2). MR hits the Q-axis at Q = 5 (half of demand's Q = 10).

3. MR & Elasticity at P = 10
\[ Q = 10 - 0.5(10) = 5 \] \[ MR = 20 - 4(5) = 0 \] \[ |\varepsilon| = 0.5 \times \frac{10}{5} = 1 \quad\text{(unit elastic at the midpoint)} \]

Profit Maximization · Summary

Revenue Maximization

Calculus tells us that revenue is maximized when marginal revenue is zero (\( MR = 0 \)).

In the case of linear demand, this occurs exactly at the midpoint of the demand curve, where \( |\varepsilon| = 1 \) (unit-elastic).

Profit Maximization · Summary

Summary: Elasticity, MR, and TR

To summarize:

  • Elastic Region (\( |\varepsilon| > 1 \)): MR is positive; lowering price increases total revenue (TR).
  • Unit-Elastic Point (\( |\varepsilon| = 1 \)): MR is zero; TR is maximized.
  • Inelastic Region (\( |\varepsilon| < 1 \)): MR is negative; lowering price decreases TR.

Firms & Markets · Introduction

Firms Maximize Profits, Not Revenues

\[ \Pi = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Total Costs} \]

Because firms choose quantity to maximize profit — not just revenue — we now combine demand, marginal revenue, and costs to analyze how a firm sets price and quantity in a market.

Monopoly · Introduction

The Monopolist's Environment

When there is no competition in a market, the market structure is a monopoly:

Market Demand = Firm Demand
The monopolist captures all of market demand. The firm does not share the market with other competitors.

Consequently, we can use the tools of consumer demand and price elasticity to understand how the monopolist thinks about consumers!

Monopoly · Decision-Making

The Decision Rule for Monopolists

There is now only one firm capturing the entire market demand for a product.

  • Market demand = demand that the monopolist faces.
Fundamental Decision Rule
What is the fundamental decision rule for profit-maximizing firms? And what does this rule decide — quantity, or price?
MR = MC

Monopoly · Decision-Making

Quantity first, then Price

How the Monopolist Operates:

  1. Find the Quantity (\( Q^* \)): The firm uses the marginal condition \( MR = MC \) to determine the optimal quantity to produce.
  2. Determine the Price (\( P^* \)): Once \( Q^* \) is chosen, the monopolist finds the highest price consumers are willing to pay for that quantity.
    This price is read from the demand curve: \( P^* = P(Q^*) \).
Crucial Intuition
The monopolist does not set an arbitrary price. They are constrained by consumer demand!

Monopoly · Example 1

Monopoly Calculation

Problem Statement
A monopolist faces demand for their product: \[ Q_D = 14 - 3P_D \] They have a constant marginal cost function: \[ MC(Q) = 2 \] Determine their profit-maximizing quantity (to produce), price, and their profits at these choices.

What is the inverse demand function? What is the MR function?

Monopoly · Solution 1

Solution: Monopoly Calculation

Step 1: Inverse Demand & MR
\[ Q_D = 14 - 3P_D \implies P = \frac{14}{3} - \frac{Q}{3} \] \[ R(Q) = \frac{14}{3}Q - \frac{Q^2}{3} \implies MR(Q) = \frac{14}{3} - \frac{2Q}{3} \]
Step 2: Set MR = MC
\[ \frac{14}{3} - \frac{2Q}{3} = 2 \implies \frac{8}{3} = \frac{2Q}{3} \implies Q^* = 4 \]
Step 3: Price & Profit
\[ P^* = \frac{14}{3} - \frac{4}{3} = \frac{10}{3} \approx 3.33 \] \[ \Pi = P^*Q^* - TC = \tfrac{10}{3}(4) - 2(4) = \tfrac{40}{3} - 8 = \tfrac{16}{3} \approx 5.33 \]

Monopoly · Example 2

Monopoly Redux

Problem Statement
A monopolist faces demand for their product: \[ P_D = 10 - 2Q_D \] They have a variable marginal cost function: \[ MC(Q) = 2Q \] Determine their profit-maximizing quantity (to produce), price, and their profits at these choices.

Solve for the quantity where \( MR = MC \), and find the corresponding price on the demand curve.

Monopoly · Solution 2

Solution: Monopoly Redux

Step 1: Inverse Demand & MR
\[ P = 10 - 2Q \] \[ R(Q) = P \cdot Q = 10Q - 2Q^2 \] \[ MR(Q) = 10 - 4Q \]
Step 2: Set MR = MC
\[ 10 - 4Q = 2Q \implies 10 = 6Q \] \[ Q^* = \frac{5}{3} \approx 1.67 \]
Step 3: Price
\[ P^* = 10 - 2\left(\frac{5}{3}\right) = \frac{20}{3} \approx 6.67 \]
