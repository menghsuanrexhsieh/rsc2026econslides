Surplus · Consumer

Defining Consumer Surplus

How do we measure consumer well-being?

  • Competitive structure directly determines the market price.
  • Each consumer has their own willingness-to-pay (valuation) for a product.
  • Market price determines the welfare they receive from transacting.
Definition
Consumer surplus (CS) is the difference between total willingness to pay and total expenditure (for a given level of consumption).

Surplus · Consumer

Willingness to Pay vs. Expenditure

Willingness to Pay (WTP)
Willingness to pay is represented by the demand curve because the curve shows the maximum price a consumer is willing to pay for each incremental unit.
Total Expenditure
Total expenditure is how much it actually costs to purchase those units: \[ \text{Total Expenditure} = P^* \times Q^* \]

Let's look at this concept graphically on the next slide!

Surplus · Warm-Up

Warm-Up: Surplus from One Purchase

Example
Rex wants to buy a Nintendo Switch 2. He is willing to pay $600 for a unit. The MSRP is $500.

What is his surplus if he buys one unit of Nintendo Switch 2?
Answer
\[ CS = \text{WTP} - \text{Expenditure} = 600 - 500 = \$100 \]

Surplus · Consumer

Visualizing Consumer Surplus

Graphical Representation:

  • Willingness to Pay: Area under the demand curve up to quantity consumed.
  • Expenditure: Rectangular area \( P^* \times Q^* \).
  • Consumer Surplus: Area below the demand curve and above the market price line.

Surplus · Consumer

Elasticity Matters for Consumer Surplus

For a fixed market price, how elastic demand is has direct implications for the size of consumer surplus!

  • Relatively inelastic (steeper) demand: consumers value inframarginal units far above the price ⇒ a larger CS area.
  • Relatively elastic (flatter) demand: valuations sit close to the price ⇒ a smaller CS area.

Let’s draw the two cases on the board to see this!

Surplus · Practice

Practice: Consumer Surplus

Problem Statement
A consumer has demand function for packs of gum given by: \[ Q_D = 30 - 5P \] The market price for gum is currently $5 per pack.

Determine how many packs of gum this consumer would consume, and calculate their consumer surplus.

Surplus · Solution

Solution: Consumer Surplus

Step 1: Find Quantity Demanded
Plugging \( P = 5 \) into the demand function: \[ Q_D = 30 - 5(5) = 5 \text{ packs} \]
Step 2: Find the Demand Choke Price
Find the price where demand is exactly zero: \[ 30 - 5P = 0 \implies P_{\text{choke}} = \$6 \]
Step 3: Calculate the Triangle Area
CS is the area of the triangle below demand and above price (\( P = 5 \Delta P \)): \[ CS = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times Q_D \times (P_{\text{choke}} - P) \] \[ CS = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times (6 - 5) = \$2.50 \]

Surplus · Producer

Defining Producer Surplus

How do we measure producer well-being?

  • Each producer has their own supply curve, representing their willingness to produce/sell at different prices.
  • The market price determines the revenue they earn from selling.
Definition
Producer surplus (PS) is the difference between total revenue and total cost/expenditure (for a given level of production).

Graphically, it is the area above the supply curve and below the price line.

Surplus · Warm-Up

Warm-Up: Surplus from One Sale

Example
Nintendo is selling one unit of Nintendo Switch 2 to Rex at $500. Nintendo’s manufacturing cost for one unit of Switch 2 is $338.

What is Nintendo’s surplus if they sell him one unit of Nintendo Switch 2?
Answer
\[ PS = \text{Revenue} - \text{Cost} = 500 - 338 = \$162 \]

Surplus · Practice

Practice: Producer Surplus

Problem Statement
A firm has supply function for packs of gum given by: \[ Q_S = 10 + 5P_S \] Suppose the firm sells each pack of gum at the market price of $5.

Determine the producer surplus for this firm.

Surplus · Solution

Solution: Producer Surplus

Step 1: Find Quantity Supplied
Plugging \( P = 5 \) into the supply function: \[ Q_S = 10 + 5(5) = 35 \text{ packs} \]
Step 2: Understand the Area
Supply starts at \( Q = 10 \) when \( P = 0 \). The area above the supply curve and below \( P = 5 \) is a trapezoid with height \( 5 \) (from \( P = 0 \) to \( P = 5 \)) and parallel bases of length \( 10 \) and \( 35 \).
Step 3: Calculate the Trapezoid Area
\[ PS = \frac{\text{base}_1 + \text{base}_2}{2} \times \text{height} = \frac{10 + 35}{2} \times 5 = 22.5 \times 5 = \$112.50 \]

Alternatively, integrate \( Q_S(P) \): \( \int_0^5 (10 + 5P) dP = [10P + 2.5P^2]_0^5 = 50 + 62.5 = \$112.50 \).

Surplus · Market Level

Practice: Market Surplus

Problem Statement
In the market for gum, market demand is given by: \[ Q_D = 30 - 5P_D \] and market supply is given by: \[ Q_S = 10 + 5P_S \]
Calculate the market equilibrium price and quantity, and then determine both the consumer surplus and producer surplus in equilibrium.

Surplus · Solution

Solution: Market Surplus

Step 1: Equilibrium \( P^* \) and \( Q^* \)
Set \( Q_D = Q_S \implies 30 - 5P = 10 + 5P \implies 20 = 10P \implies P^* = \$2 \).
Plugging back in: \( Q^* = 30 - 5(2) = 20 \) units.
Step 2: Consumer Surplus
Choke price is \( P_{\text{choke}} = \$6 \). \[ CS = \frac{1}{2} \times Q^* \times (P_{\text{choke}} - P^*) = \frac{1}{2} \times 20 \times (6 - 2) = \$40 \]
Step 3: Producer Surplus
Supply curve starts at \( Q = 10 \) at \( P = 0 \). The area below \( P^* = 2 \) is a trapezoid: \[ PS = \frac{10 + 20}{2} \times 2 = 15 \times 2 = \$30 \]
Step 4: Total Social Welfare
\[ W = CS + PS = 40 + 30 = \$70 \]

Welfare · Definition

Defining Social Welfare

Definition
Social welfare (or social surplus) is the sum of consumer surplus and producer surplus in the market: \[ W = CS + PS \]
  • Social welfare/surplus is maximized under perfect competition.
  • Monopolist pricing reduces social surplus.
Definition: Efficiency
A market is said to be efficient if there is no deadweight loss (or, when social surplus is maximized).

Welfare · Deadweight Loss

Welfare Loss from Market Shocks

What happens when markets deviate from equilibrium?

  • Quantities transacted decrease.
  • Some consumer and producer surplus is lost.
  • The area C represents lost surplus that is not transferred to anyone.

Welfare · Deadweight Loss

Defining Deadweight Loss

Definition
Deadweight loss (DWL) is the net reduction in total surplus from an event, action, or policy that alters the market equilibrium, resulting in a loss of social surplus that is not offset by a gain to another group.

Why does it occur?

  • Consumers value extra output by more than the marginal cost of producing it.
  • By restricting trade, society misses out on mutually beneficial transactions.

Welfare · Market Power

Welfare Under Market Power

Recall Monopoly Pricing:

  • Monopolists set price above MC, restricting output to maximize profits.
  • This pricing behavior causes a transfer of surplus from consumers to the firm.
  • It also creates a deadweight loss (Area C), reducing total social welfare.

Welfare · Practice

Practice: DWL from Market Power

Problem Statement
Consider the iPad market in the figure. Under competition, \( P_c = \$200 \) (equal to MC) and \( Q_c = 160 \)M. With full market power, the monopolist sets \( P_m = \$600 \) and \( Q_m = 80 \)M.

What is the deadweight loss that results from full market power (i.e., monopolist pricing)?

Welfare · Solution

Solution: DWL from Market Power

Step 1: Identify the DWL Triangle (Area C)
The lost transactions run from \( Q_m = 80 \)M to \( Q_c = 160 \)M; the wedge at \( Q_m \) is the gap between the monopoly price and marginal cost: \( P_m - P_c = 600 - 200 = \$400 \).
Step 2: Calculate the Triangle
\[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times (Q_c - Q_m) \times (P_m - P_c) = \frac{1}{2} \times 80\text{M} \times 400 = \$16{,}000\text{M} \]

DWL = $16 billion — surplus lost to society, transferred to no one.

Welfare · Deadweight Loss

How Else Might Deadweight Loss Arise?

A natural follow-up question is: how else might deadweight loss arise?

The Common Theme
There are many possibilities, but all of them share the same theme: some party (or parties) is “hurt” as a result of the event, action, or policy change.

Welfare · Deadweight Loss

The Inefficiencies of Christmas

Case Study: Gift-Giving Economics (The Economist)
Joel Waldfogel (1993) identified a “deadweight loss” of Christmas: the gap between what seasonal gifts cost and how much recipients actually value them.
  • Cash spent on another person yields only about 85% of the benefit of cash people spend on themselves.
  • One estimate: unwanted gifts destroyed around £3bn of the £25bn spent on gifts by British adults in 2021.
  • Gift-giving may make people happy — but as an allocation mechanism, it is “a lousy way to allocate resources.”

Welfare · Shocks

Practice: Welfare Effects of a Shock

Problem Statement
The market for Nintendo Switch 2 is in equilibrium. However, supply chain issues doubled the costs of production.

Perform a welfare analysis, graphically, that shows the impact of this shock. Assume market demand remains unchanged.

Welfare · Solution

Solution: Welfare Effects of a Shock

Step 1: Shift the Supply Curve
Doubled production costs shift the supply curve up/left (each quantity now requires a higher price to be supplied). Demand stays put.
Step 2: New Equilibrium
The equilibrium price rises and the equilibrium quantity falls.
Step 3: Welfare Comparison
Consumer surplus shrinks (higher price, fewer units), and total surplus falls relative to the pre-shock market. Some previously beneficial transactions no longer happen — consumers and producers are both hurt by the shock.

Interventions · Rationale

Why Intervene in Markets?

Market efficiency relies on several critical assumptions:

  1. Income Distribution: Competitive markets maximize total surplus given the initial distribution of income, which might be unequal or undesirable.
  2. Consumer Sovereignty: Consumers are assumed to be the best judges of their own utility.
  3. No Externalities: Producers must bear all production costs, and purchasers must receive all benefits.

If any of these assumptions fail, government intervention may be economically justified to "generate desirable market outcomes."

Interventions · Price Controls

Price Ceilings & Price Floors

Definition
Price controls are legislation that guarantees market prices — preventing prices from moving to equilibrium.
  • Price Ceiling (\( p^1 \)): A legal maximum price (e.g. rent control).
  • Price Floor (\( p^0 \)): A legal minimum price (e.g. minimum wage).

Questions: What is the effect of a price ceiling? Of a price floor? How much is actually traded at these prices?

Interventions · Price Floor

Policy Example: Minimum Wage

Welfare Effects of a Price Floor:

  • A minimum wage sets a legal minimum price in the labor market.
  • If set above the market equilibrium wage, it increases the quantity of labor supplied (more people want to work) but reduces the quantity of labor demanded (firms hire fewer workers).
  • This discrepancy creates a surplus of labor (unemployment) and generates a deadweight loss for society.

Interventions · Price Ceiling

Practice: Rent Control

Problem Statement
A rent control policy setting \( \$1500 \) per month rent on apartments in San Francisco is being considered. Assume apartments are identical, with demand given by: \[ P = 3600 - Q \] and supply given by: \[ P = 400 + Q \]
1. Calculate the deadweight loss due to this price ceiling.
2. If supply were perfectly inelastic at the equilibrium point, would the deadweight loss be smaller or larger?

Interventions · Solution

Solution: Rent Control

Step 1: Find Market Equilibrium (No Intervention)
\[ 3600 - Q = 400 + Q \implies 3200 = 2Q \implies Q^* = 1600, P^* = \$2000 \]
Step 2: Apply Price Ceiling (\( P_c = 1500 \))
At \( P = 1500 \), supply is binding: \( Q_S = 1500 - 400 = 1100 \) units (Shortage: \( Q_D - Q_S = 2100 - 1100 = 1000 \)).
Step 3: Calculate Deadweight Loss (DWL)
At \( Q = 1100 \), demand valuation is \( P_D = 3600 - 1100 = 2500 \); MC is \( P_S = 1500 \). \[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times (Q^* - Q_S) \times (P_D - P_S) = \frac{1}{2} \times (1600 - 1100) \times (2500 - 1500) \] \[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times 500 \times 1000 = \$250,000 \]
Part 2: Inelastic Supply
If supply is perfectly inelastic at \( Q = 1600 \), quantity remains unchanged at 1600. No output is lost, so DWL is zero (which is smaller).

Interventions · Taxes

Prices Controlled by Taxes

How do taxes affect market outcomes?

  • Governments artificially change transaction prices by introducing per-unit or percentage taxes.
  • A tax creates a wedge between what consumers pay (\( P_b \)) and what sellers receive (\( P_s \)).
  • This tax wedge restricts the quantity traded and creates deadweight loss.
Tax Identity
\[ P_b = P_s + t \] where \( t \) is the per-unit tax rate.

Interventions · Tax Example

Practice: Producer Tax

Problem Statement
Suppose supply and demand for a competitively supplied product are given as: \[ Q_D = 150 - 25P \quad\text{or}\quad P = 6 - 0.04Q_D \] \[ Q_S = 50P \quad\text{or}\quad P = 0.02Q_S \]
1. Find the equilibrium market price and quantity.
2. The government imposes a per-unit tax of $0.30 on suppliers. Calculate:
  • New equilibrium price paid by consumers (\( P_b \)).
  • Price received by sellers (\( P_s \)).
  • Quantity transacted (\( Q_{\text{tax}} \)).
  • The tax incidence (portion paid by consumers).

Interventions · Solution

Solution: Producer Tax

Step 1: Equilibrium without Tax
\[ 150 - 25P = 50P \implies 75P = 150 \implies P^* = \$2.00, Q^* = 100 \text{ units} \]
Step 2: Solve with Tax wedge (\( P_b = P_s + 0.30 \))
Substitute buyers' price into demand: \[ 150 - 25(P_s + 0.30) = 50P_s \implies 150 - 25P_s - 7.5 = 50P_s \] \[ 142.5 = 75P_s \implies P_s = \$1.90 \implies P_b = 1.90 + 0.30 = \$2.20 \]
Step 3: Quantity & Tax Incidence
Quantity transacted: \( Q_{\text{tax}} = 50(1.90) = 95 \) units.
Price rise for consumers: \( \Delta P_b = 2.20 - 2.00 = \$0.20 \).
Consumers' portion: \( \frac{\Delta P_b}{t} = \frac{0.20}{0.30} = \frac{2}{3} \approx 66.7\% \).

Interventions · Tax Burden

Tax Incidence & Elasticities

Key Principle
It does not matter on whom the tax is legally imposed; the economic burden (incidence) depends entirely on the relative elasticities of supply and demand.

Economic Incidence Formulas:

  • Incidence on Buyers: \( \text{Burden}_C = \frac{\varepsilon_S}{\varepsilon_S + |\varepsilon_D|} \)
  • Incidence on Sellers: \( \text{Burden}_P = \frac{|\varepsilon_D|}{\varepsilon_S + |\varepsilon_D|} \)

The side of the market that is more inelastic (less responsive to price changes) bears the larger share of the tax burden.

Interventions · Tax Welfare

Practice: Tax Welfare Effects

Problem Statement
Using the same supply and demand model: \[ Q_D = 150 - 25P \] \[ Q_S = 50P \] where a per-unit tax of $0.30 is imposed.

Calculate the deadweight loss (DWL) resulting from this tax.

Interventions · Solution

Solution: Tax Deadweight Loss

Step 1: Identify the Quantity reduction
Equilibrium quantity fell from \( Q^* = 100 \) to \( Q_{\text{tax}} = 95 \) units. \[ \Delta Q = 100 - 95 = 5 \text{ units} \]
Step 2: Identify the height (tax wedge)
The vertical wedge between the demand price and supply price is the tax: \[ t = \$0.30 \]
Step 3: Calculate the DWL Triangle
\[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times \text{base} \times \text{height} = \frac{1}{2} \times \Delta Q \times t \] \[ DWL = \frac{1}{2} \times 5 \times 0.30 = \$0.75 \]

Interventions · Taxes

Why Do Taxes Generate DWL?

The Key Insight
Taxes generate deadweight loss because taxes affect relative prices — and therefore change behavior!
  • The only tax that does not change prices is a lump-sum tax (a fixed amount, independent of any choice you make).
  • Side note: taxes affect our decisions! E.g. marriage, migration, location of investment, residential choices, having children…
  • This is covered in depth in your ECON 101 class — and a decent proportion of Rex’s own research measures behavioral responses to tax policy changes (the elasticity of taxable income).

Interventions · Taxes

Taxes Even Affect the Timing of Death!

  • Kopczuk & Slemrod use U.S. federal estate-tax returns to ask whether the timing of death responds to its tax consequences.
  • Central estimate: for individuals dying within two weeks of a tax reform, a $10,000 potential tax saving increases the probability of dying in the lower-tax regime by 1.6%.
  • Taxes affect behavior — even the timing of behaviors we don’t usually think respond to financial incentives!

Summary

Welfare and Efficiency

If competitive assumptions are met, market equilibrium is efficient:

  • Production costs are minimized.
  • The sum of consumer and producer surplus is maximized.
Policy Takeaway
In the absence of market failures, government interference with the competitive equilibrium via price controls or taxes leads to deadweight loss.
