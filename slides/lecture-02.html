Supply and Demand · Recap

We Left Off Here: Market Equilibrium

Definition
The market equilibrium is where supply meets demand. Any equilibrium consists of an equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity.

Supply and Demand · Recap

Equilibrium as a Snapshot

Remember markets are defined for a product, at a particular place, at a point in time.

Therefore, any equilibrium describes a snapshot of individual consumers’ and firms’ behavior at a given time.

What if:

  • There are fewer consumers?
  • All firms experience a cost increase due to tariffs?

Supply and Demand · Recap

Movements Along Curves

  • If only the price of a product goes up (everything else held constant), then a consumer would want to consume less.
  • Likewise, if only the price of a product a firm can sell at goes up (everything else held constant), then a producer is willing to produce more.
  • These statements are equivalent to movement along the supply/demand curves.

What if there are more than price changes?

Supply and Demand · Recap

Shifts of the Curves

In life, there are many changes that are not related to the price of the product changing.

Examples of External Changes
Extreme climate events, limited supply of raw materials, shipping delays, shrinking customer base, etc.
  • If there are changes to any factors other than price that affect demand, then the entire demand curve shifts.
  • If there are changes to any factors other than price that affect supply, then the entire supply curve shifts.

But be cautious about the market that we are analyzing!

Algebra in supply & demand

Shocks vs. Movements

In economic analysis, it is critical to distinguish between these effects:

Definition: Shock
Any change to market conditions is called a shock.
  • Market equilibrium changes only if a shock occurs that shifts the demand or supply curve.
  • Demand or supply curve shifts occur if variables we were holding constant (tastes, income, government policies, costs of production) change.
  • Movement along the demand or supply curve is a result of only price changes.

With our understanding of supply and demand: we now know that each point on the supply and demand curves describes a particular willingness-to-produce and willingness-to-pay.

We can ask: How can we quantify behavior at various points along the supply and demand curves?

The Solution
The correct concept to use is called Elasticity.

Elasticity

What is Elasticity?

Elasticity is a statement about changes to behavior when price changes.

It measures how responsive consumers or producers are to changes in market conditions.

Elasticity · Definition

(Own-Price) Elasticity of Demand

Definition
The elasticity of demand is the ratio of the percentage change in quantity to the percentage change in price. (Also called point elasticity of demand).

We define the own-price elasticity of demand \( \varepsilon \) as:

\[ (1) \quad \varepsilon = \frac{\% \Delta Q}{\% \Delta P} \]
\[ (2) \quad \varepsilon = \frac{\Delta Q / Q}{\Delta P / P} = \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \times \frac{P}{Q} \]

Where \( \frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P} \) is the derivative (slope) of the demand function.
By definition, \( \varepsilon \) is negative, but we follow tradition and work with the absolute value \( |\varepsilon| \).

Elasticity · Example

  • Horizontal intercept: \( (Q=10, P=0) \)
  • Vertical intercept: \( (Q=0, P=20) \)

Elasticity · Midpoint Rule

Elasticity Along a Linear Demand Curve

Elasticity regions on a linear curve:

  • Elastic above the midpoint: \( |\varepsilon| > 1 \)
  • Unit-elastic at the midpoint: \( |\varepsilon| = 1 \)
  • Inelastic below the midpoint: \( |\varepsilon| < 1 \)

Elasticity · Classification

Relatively Elastic vs. Inelastic

Definition: Relatively Elastic
Demand is relatively elastic if, for a given percentage change in price, the resulting percentage change in quantity demanded is large.
Definition: Relatively Inelastic
Demand is relatively inelastic if, for a given percentage change in price, the resulting percentage change in quantity demanded is small.

Other elasticity measures

Income & Cross-Price Elasticities

Definition: Income Elasticity
For any good \( X \), if the income elasticity of demand for \( X \) is:
  • Negative: then \( X \) is an inferior good.
  • Positive: then \( X \) is a normal good.
Definition: Cross-Price Elasticity
For any two goods \( X \) and \( Y \), if the cross-price elasticity of demand for \( X \) with respect to the price of \( Y \) is:
  • Negative: then \( X \) and \( Y \) are complements.
  • Positive: then \( X \) and \( Y \) are substitutes.

Elasticity & Marginal Revenue

The Unit-Elastic Midpoint Rule

Key Fact
For a firm facing a given demand function, the firm’s marginal revenue is zero when they set a price where the customer’s demand is unit-elastic (\( |\varepsilon| = 1 \)).

This represents the peak of total revenue (midpoint for linear demand). This is our first foray into analyzing market behavior!

Elasticity & Marginal Revenue

What is Marginal Revenue?

Definition
Marginal revenue (MR) is the incremental revenue made by firms when they produce one additional unit of product.

Mathematically, marginal revenue is the derivative of the total revenue (TR) function with respect to quantity:

\[ MR = \frac{d(TR)}{dQ} \]

This is the concept of marginal benefits for firms!
