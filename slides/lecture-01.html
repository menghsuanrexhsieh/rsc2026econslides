What is economics?

The Study of Choices

Microeconomics is, very broadly, the study of decision-making.

  • Making a decision means one alternative is selected over another ⇒ trade-offs are incurred.
  • To understand how decisions are made, we need to analyze:
    • Scarcity + Choice
    • Opportunity Cost
  • By understanding these three ingredients ⇒ we can start analyzing intriguing phenomena that we observe in the real world.

This class is about learning the fundamental tools that go into economic analyses.

What is economics?

Scarcity

Definition
A scarce good is one for which the choice of one alternative requires that another be given up.

Scarcity is associated with three fundamental questions:

  1. What should be produced?
  2. How should goods and services be produced?
  3. For whom should goods and services be produced?

What is economics?

Opportunity Cost

Definition
Opportunity cost is the value of the best alternative forgone in making any choice.

How to make economic decisions?

Maximizing Net Benefit

Scarcity implies trade-offs exist: opportunity costs are (generally) nonzero.

Rational Choice Assumption
Economists assume individuals maximize the net benefit of each potential decision: \[ \text{Net Benefit} = \text{Total Benefit} - \text{Opportunity Costs} \]

What do we use to characterize how these decisions are made?

How to make economic decisions?

Thinking on the Margin

Answer: thinking on the margin.

We need to look at the additional benefit and the additional cost of another unit of the activity.

Definition: Marginal Benefit
The marginal benefit (MB) is the amount by which an additional unit of an activity increases an individual’s total benefit.
Definition: Marginal Cost
The marginal cost (MC) is the amount by which an additional unit of an activity increases an individual’s total cost.

How to make economic decisions?

The Decision Rule

Decision rule: we want to make a decision where the marginal benefit equals marginal cost.

Specifically, think about the marginal decision of doing an activity:

  • If MB > MC, then do more: → ↑ Q
  • If MB < MC, then do less: → ↓ Q
Why? Spotify Example
Imagine buying a subscription to Spotify, which is an all-or-nothing decision. Another reason why we think this way: Calculus (which we will see later!).

How to make economic decisions?

The Core Principle

We will spend a lot of time with the following decision rule:

MB = MC

Individuals and firms make decisions at which marginal benefit equals marginal cost.

Please remember this decision rule!

We now begin translating this fundamental decision into objects that are important in economics.

Demand

We begin with demand.

Definition
Demand for a good or service is the amount of a good that a consumer is willing to buy at a given price.

Supply and Demand · Demand

Markets & Demand

When we talk about demand, there is always a market.

Definition
A market refers to a specific product being bought and sold at a given quantity, at a specific location, and at a given point in time.

Therefore, “my demand” always refers to a specific product or other commodities and services. My demand for a product is equivalent to saying what quantity of a product I want.

Supply

We now define supply.

Definition
Supply of a product is the quantity of the good that a producer is willing to (produce and) sell.

It is important to emphasize “willing to sell” in the definition, and we will see this is a function of costs.

Supply and Demand Together

Model Assumptions

Together, supply and demand form the basic working model of modern economics.

Assumptions for this model to be meaningful for analyses:

  1. Focuses on a single market with many buyers and sellers.
  2. All goods/services sold in the market are identical.
  3. All goods/services sold in the market are transacted at the same price.
  4. Everyone has the same information about prices, quality of goods/services, etc.

Supply and Demand Together

Market Equilibrium

Definition
The market equilibrium is where supply meets demand. Any equilibrium consists of an equilibrium price and equilibrium quantity.

Supply and Demand Together

Equilibrium as a Snapshot

Remember markets are defined for a product, at a particular place, at a point in time.

Therefore, any equilibrium describes a snapshot of individual consumers’ and firms’ behavior at a given time.

What if:

  • There are fewer consumers?
  • Firms get less raw materials for production because of extreme weather events?

Supply and Demand Together

Movements Along Curves

  • If only the price of a product goes up (everything else held constant), then a consumer would want to consume less.
  • Likewise, if only the price of a product a firm can sell at goes up (everything else held constant), then a producer is willing to produce more.
  • These statements are equivalent to movement along the supply/demand curves.
  • These statements are also equivalent to saying ceteris paribus (all else held constant, a price change...).

What if there are more than price changes?

Supply and Demand Together

Shifts of the Curves

In life, there are many changes that are not related to the price of the product changing.

Examples of External Changes
Extreme climate events, limited supply of raw materials, shipping delays, shrinking customer base, etc.
Core Rule: Shifts
If there are changes to any factors other than price that affect demand/supply, then the entire demand/supply curve shifts.
