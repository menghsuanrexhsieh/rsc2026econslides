Preliminary Definitions · Players (1/5)

Players

Definition: Players
All games need players. Players are economic agents — e.g. friends, consumers, firms, the government, or Nature.

(Yes, even the weather can be a player — we’ll see this shortly!)

Preliminary Definitions · Actions (2/5)

Actions & Payoffs

Definition: Actions
All players can play some number of actions — e.g. “move left”, “move right”, “pay a fine”, “don’t enter a market”.

Each player’s actions come with rewards (positive payoff) or “punishment” (negative payoff).

Preliminary Definitions · Strategies (3/5)

Strategies as Contingency Plans

Each player is rational (enough) to have contingency plans.

Example: It’s Me Against the World
Rex plays a game with Nature. He has the actions: (1) bring an umbrella, (2) not bring an umbrella. Nature has the actions: (1) make it sunny in Ann Arbor, (2) make it rain in Ann Arbor.

Rex’s contingency plans include (but are not limited to): bring an umbrella if it rains; bring an umbrella if it is sunny; …
Definition: Strategy
A player’s strategy is their entire collection of contingency plans. For this class, we use strategies and actions interchangeably (don’t worry about the distinction!).

Preliminary Definitions · Payoffs (4/5)

Payoffs and the Normal Form

Definition: Payoff & Normal Form
A payoff is an outcome of a game that depends on the strategies every player selects. We represent players, actions/strategies, and payoffs in the normal form of a game:
Rex (Player 2)
Bring Umbrella (B)Don’t Bring Umbrella (D)
Nature
(Player 1)		Rain (R)2, 43, 2
No Rain (N)2.5, 1.53.5, 0

In each cell: Player 1’s payoff first, Player 2’s payoff second — e.g. “3, 2” means Nature gets 3 and Rex gets 2.

Preliminary Definitions · Rules (5/5)

Simultaneous-Move, One-Shot Games

Players, actions, strategies, payoffs give the basic structure of a game. We still need rules.

Example, continued
Say Rex and Nature both decide what to play at the same time, and they play this game once: a simultaneous-move, one-shot game.
Definitions
A simultaneous-move game is a game where players choose their strategies at the same time. A one-shot game is where players play the game exactly once.

Equilibrium Concepts · Dominance (1/11)

Dominant & Dominated Strategies

First answer to Question 1: imagine everyone has a strategy that gives the highest possible payoff, regardless of what anyone else does. This is the idea of dominance.

Definition: Dominant Strategy
A player’s dominant strategy gives the highest possible payoff, regardless of what anyone else does.
Definition: Dominated Strategy
A player’s dominated strategy does not give the highest possible payoff, regardless of what anyone else does.

In Rex’s game with Nature, do the players have dominant strategies?

Equilibrium Concepts · Dominance (2/11)

Eliminating Dominated Strategies

Step 2
Move onto player 2 (Rex; payoffs listed second). Does one of his strategies always give lower payoffs for himself? If yes, delete it.

Equilibrium Concepts · DSE (3/11)

Dominant Strategy Equilibrium

Definition: Dominant Strategy Equilibrium (DSE)
A dominant strategy equilibrium (DSE) is an equilibrium where every player plays their dominant strategy (if it exists for every player).

In Rex’s game with Nature, the DSE is (No Rain, Bring Umbrella) with payoffs (2.5, 1.5).

Equilibrium Concepts · DSE (4–5/11)

Two Conceptual Questions

Conceptual Question #1: Is there always a dominant strategy?
No! So, a DSE does not always exist.
Conceptual Question #2: Is a dominant strategy always “good”?
No, not always! In the umbrella game, the outcome (Rain, Don’t Bring) would give Nature 3 > 2.5 — playing dominant strategies isn’t always the “most optimal” outcome.

Takeaway: DSE is a very strong definition of equilibrium. It often does not exist, and is not always the “most optimal” equilibrium even when it does.

Equilibrium Concepts · Nash Equilibrium (6/11)

Best-Responses & Nash Equilibrium

Updated answer to Question 1: “zoom out” from DSE — everyone has a strategy that gives the highest possible payoff, but only conditional on the strategies other players are playing.

Definition: Best-Response
A player’s best-response is the strategy that gives the highest possible payoff to the player — taking all other players’ strategies as given.
Definition: Nash Equilibrium (NE)
A Nash equilibrium (NE) is an equilibrium where every player plays best-responses (or, equivalently, plays mutual best-responses).

Equilibrium Concepts · Nash Equilibrium (7/11)

Steps for Finding NE

Step 1
Fix player 2’s strategy. If Rex plays “B”, which of Nature’s strategies gives it a higher payoff? Repeat for Rex playing “D”.
Step 2
Fix player 1’s strategy. If Nature plays “R”, which of Rex’s strategies gives him a higher payoff? Repeat for Nature playing “N”.

A cell where both players are best-responding is a Nash equilibrium: here, (N, B) — the same as the DSE!

Rex (Player 2)
Bring Umbrella (B)Don’t Bring Umbrella (D)
Nature
(Player 1)		Rain (R)2, 43, 2
No Rain (N)2.5, 1.53.5, 0

Equilibrium Concepts · Nash Equilibrium (8/11)

DSE vs. NE

It’s not a coincidence that the DSE and NE coincide in this game!

  • A DSE is always a NE!
  • But a NE is not always a DSE!
Jargon
DSE is a stronger/stricter definition of an equilibrium. We say DSE is a refinement of NE.

Equilibrium Concepts · Jargon (9/11)

NE in Pure Strategies

Definition: Pure Strategy
A pure strategy is a strategy in which a player chooses a particular action with certainty. We’ve defined NE in pure strategies; a DSE is always in pure strategies.
Remark (Rex’s unconventional POV!)
We ought to understand NE as the “end point” of a “learning process”: each player chooses the optimal action, assuming all other players choose the same actions now as they did in the previous time period.

Equilibrium Concepts · Jargon (10/11)

NE in Mixed Strategies

Definition: Mixed Strategy
A mixed strategy is a strategy in which a player randomizes over a particular collection of actions.

We won’t bother with NE in mixed strategies for this class.

Question 2: Is a NE always the best outcome for the players, normatively? I.e., does a NE always give the highest possible payoffs to all players?

Equilibrium Concepts · Prisoner’s Dilemma (11/11)

The Prisoner’s Dilemma

Definition: Prisoner’s Dilemma
A prisoner’s dilemma is a situation where the Nash equilibrium outcome is worse off for all players in the game — worse compared to if they deviate from the strategies they play in the Nash equilibrium.

Is Rex’s game with Nature an example of a prisoner’s dilemma? We’ll see clean examples of true PDs shortly!

Equilibrium Concepts · One-Sided Dominance

Lesson from the Entry Game

Lesson for HBO Max (and for any player who doesn’t have a dominant strategy):

  • Check and see if your rival has a dominant strategy!
  • If your rival does — and assuming they behave rationally — select the strategy that maximizes your payoffs given the favored strategy of your rival.

Equilibrium Concepts · Solution 1

Solution: Theater Pricing

Step 1: Check for Dominant Strategies
For Tuppan: if AB prices High, 35 > 25; if AB prices Low, 60 > 52 — Price High is dominant. By symmetry, Price High is dominant for AB too.
Step 2: The NE
The unique NE (and DSE) is (Price High, Price High) with payoffs (35, 35).
Step 3: Can They Do Better?
Yes! Both playing Price Low gives (52, 52) > (35, 35) — the NE is worse for everyone: a textbook prisoner’s dilemma.

Strategic Commitment · Focal Points (1/5)

Which Equilibrium Actually Happens?

  • When there are multiple Nash equilibria and players cannot communicate, it is difficult to tell which one will actually occur.
  • Sometimes one equilibrium seems more likely, or is “focal”.
  • A focal point is a solution people tend to use in the absence of communication because it seems natural, obvious, or special.
Example
Guessing where we might meet up @ Ross!

Strategic Commitment · Definition (2/5)

Strategic Commitments

One way to pin down a specific equilibrium is by making a commitment.

Definition: Strategic Commitment
Strategic commitments are decisions that have long-term impacts and are difficult to reverse.
Examples
Investments in the positioning of a brand; investments in capacity; long-term contracts with suppliers or customers.

Strategic commitments can help solve coordination problems, help solve prisoner’s dilemmas, and improve outcomes (from bargaining, competition, etc.).

Strategic Commitment · Entry & Coordination (3/5)

Entry and Coordination Game

Problem Statement
Philips and Sony are making entry decisions for LED smart lightbulb introduction to the U.S. market. The payoffs are profits for each firm. Does either firm have a dominant strategy? Find all Nash equilibria.
Sony
Enter NowStay Out
PhilipsEnter Now12, 418, 5
Stay Out15, 716, 6

Strategic Commitment · Summary (4/5)

Entry Game: A Summary

  • If Sony “enters now”, Philips wants to delay and “stay out” (15 > 12).
  • If Sony waits and “stays out”, Philips wants to “enter now” (18 > 16).
  • Similarly for Sony (7 > 6 and 5 > 4): Sony wants to do the opposite of whatever Philips does.
  • Neither firm has a dominant strategy.

There are two Nash equilibria: (Enter Now, Stay Out) = (18, 5), and (Stay Out, Enter Now) = (15, 7). But these aren’t normatively the same!

Strategic Commitment · Credibility (5/5)

Starting and Building a Credible Commitment

Clearly, Philips can do better than the $15 payoff!

Question: What would Philips like to convince Sony of?
Philips would like Sony to believe that it will “Enter Now”! (Philips can’t tell Sony this explicitly — doing so is per se collusion, which is illegal.)

Question: How might Philips go about convincing Sony that it will “Enter Now”? ⇒ a credible strategic commitment (e.g., visibly investing in capacity)!

Transition

Thinking About Competition — and Fighting It

Firms with market power can:

  1. Engage in product differentiation (cf. our earlier discussions);
  2. Engage in price discrimination;
  3. Price cooperatively, i.e. tacitly collude;
  4. Fight and accommodate (of which there are many ways…).

Let’s start with point #3 — via repeated games!

Infinitely Repeated Games · 1/11

Repeated Games

Definition: Repeated Game
A repeated game is a simultaneous-move game that is played more than once by the same players.

In repeated games, players are presented with the opportunity to tacitly coordinate with one another and reach better outcomes.

By “reaching better outcomes”, we mean: the players can avoid “being plagued” by the prisoner’s dilemma.

Infinitely Repeated Games · 2/11

Philips and Sony, With New Payoffs

Sony
Enter NowStay Out
PhilipsEnter Now30, 3014, 35
Stay Out35, 1420, 20

Interesting question: is there incentive to deviate from the NE to the “better for both players” outcome — if Philips and Sony play this game forever? Assume both players know the rules, and know they will play this game forever.

(Fact: it is much easier to analyze a game with an infinite time horizon!)

Infinitely Repeated Games · 3/11

A Thought Experiment

Thought experiment. Imagine they agree to play this game, with the following rules:

  • (Cooperate) Philips agrees to play “Enter Now” forever, as long as Sony plays “Enter Now” forever.
  • (Defect) If Sony plays “Stay Out” at any point, Philips responds by playing “Stay Out” in all subsequent rounds. Sony signals to do the same.

Is this strategy sustainable? I.e., is this a strategy Sony and Philips play in equilibrium?

Sony
Enter NowStay Out
PhilipsEnter Now30, 307, 55
Stay Out55, 714, 14

Infinitely Repeated Games · 4/11

Step 0: Discounting Future Payments

Remember how we discount future payments, using interest rate \( r \):

Example
A payment of \( \$100 \), due in 1 year, is worth \( PV = \frac{100}{1+r} \) today. If \( r = 0.05 \), the present value of \( \$100 \) (due in 1 year) is \( \approx \$95.24 \) today.
Fact (Please Remember This Formula!)
The sum of all discounted PVs of \( \$100 \) paid from 1 year out until forever: \[ \frac{100}{1+r} + \frac{100}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots = \frac{100}{r} \]

We call \( r \) a discount factor in game theory.

Infinitely Repeated Games · 5/11

Step 1: PV of Income Streams — Cooperate

Case (a) — Cooperate. Philips plays “Enter Now” forever, as long as Sony plays “Enter Now” forever: both earn 30 every round.

Philips’ (and Sony’s) Total PV If They Always Cooperate
\[ 30 + \frac{30}{1+r} + \frac{30}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots = 30 + \frac{30}{r} \quad (\square) \]

Infinitely Repeated Games · 6/11

Step 2: PV of Income Streams — Defect

Case (b) — Defect. Defecting today means grabbing 55 once (playing “Stay Out” while the rival cooperates) — then both play “Stay Out” forever, earning 14 per round.

Philips’ (and Sony’s) Total PV If They Defect
\[ 55 + \frac{14}{1+r} + \frac{14}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots = 55 + \frac{14}{r} \quad (\star) \]

Philips will defect and play “Stay Out” if \( (\star) > (\square) \). The conclusion for Sony is identical, because the game is symmetric.

Infinitely Repeated Games · 7/11

Step 3: Compare the Streams

\( (\star) > (\square) \) means:

\[ 55 + \frac{14}{r} > 30 + \frac{30}{r} \]

Solving, we have:

\[ 25 > \frac{16}{r} \iff r > \frac{16}{25} = 0.64 \]
Step 4: Conclude
Philips and Sony will defect (and not cooperate!) if their discount rate is very high, i.e. if \( r > 0.64 \) — meaning they heavily discount the future (they are impatient).

Infinitely Repeated Games · 8/11

The Grim Trigger

Interpretation: so long as each firm cares enough about the future, it will cooperate — and tacitly collude — with its rival.

Definition: Grim Trigger
“‘Enter Now’ forever, unless the other player plays ‘Stay Out’ in any round” — this strategy is called a grim trigger.

The grim trigger strategy is a Nash equilibrium strategy for both players when \( r \le 0.64 \). Otherwise, Sony and Philips will find it worthwhile to “defect”. ■

Infinitely Repeated Games · 9/11

When Do We See Grim Triggers?

Grim triggers work (and can be NE!) when:

  • ✓ Cooperating is a best-response when the other player is cooperating;
  • ✓ There is an “infinite time horizon”;
  • ✓ A threat of future punishment exists, and is credible (a credible threat).

If players are not sufficiently patient (\( r > 0.64 \) in our example), defection happens — we end up with suboptimal outcomes for both players.

In general: cooperation can be sustained in most games like this — as long as players are patient enough.

Infinitely Repeated Games · 10/11

Steps to Solve an Infinitely Repeated Game

Step 1. Calculate the income streams of payoffs under the “Cooperate” and “Defect” strategies.
Step 2. Discount those income streams to the present.
Step 3. Compare the streams; figure out the discount rate under which PV of “Cooperate” > PV of “Defect”.
Step 4. Conclude the analysis by stating the discount factor under which players always cooperate (i.e., no one pulls the grim trigger).

Question 2

Group Work · Answers

Answers: Pulling the Grim Trigger

Question 1: The Two PV Streams
\[ PV_{\text{cooperate}} = 50 + \frac{50}{1+r} + \frac{50}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots = 50 + \frac{50}{r} \] \[ PV_{\text{defect}} = 90 + \frac{30}{1+r} + \frac{30}{(1+r)^2} + \cdots = 90 + \frac{30}{r} \]
Question 2: When Does Cooperation Survive?
Defect iff \( 90 + \frac{30}{r} > 50 + \frac{50}{r} \iff 40 > \frac{20}{r} \iff r > 0.5 \).

So the grim trigger sustains cooperation (is a NE) when \( r \le 0.5 \): as long as the firms are patient enough — they value future profits highly — tacit collusion holds.

Finitely Repeated Games · 1/4

The Fun Begins When There’s an End in Sight

Suppose we play the same game, but only twice.

Sony
Enter NowStay Out
PhilipsEnter Now30, 3014, 55
Stay Out55, 1420, 20

There’s now an end in sight — namely, the second round. The NE (in pure strategies) of the stage game is still (Stay Out, Stay Out).

What must happen in the second round?

Finitely Repeated Games · 2/4

Iterating the Logic

In the final round, there is no future to protect — so both players defect and play the stage-game NE (Stay Out, Stay Out).

Let’s iterate the logic: do the players have incentives to deviate from NE in the 5th round? The 10th round?

Knowing the last round ends in defection, cooperation in the second-to-last round has no reward either — and the whole game unravels back to the start!

Finitely Repeated Games · 3/4

The Endgame Problem

The Endgame Problem
If there is a definite end, then both players know there is no benefit to cooperating in the last round (i.e., they “Stay Out” in the final round).
  • We say finite games suffer from non-credible threats: the threat of pulling the grim trigger has no “bite” in finite games.
  • By extension, threats and promises are also not credible in the second-to-last round, third-to-last round, etc.

Finitely Repeated Games · 4/4

How to Solve Finite Games: Backwards Induction

Finite games have an endgame. Let’s start from the end, and work backwards.

Definition: Backwards Induction
Backwards induction is the way to solve finitely repeated games: start at the end of the game and work backwards in “time” to figure out the equilibrium strategies and payoffs.

It’s easier to illustrate backwards induction in sequential-move games — the subject of next class!

Recap

Class 6 Summary

  • Games = players + actions/strategies + payoffs + rules; represented in normal form.
  • DSE (everyone plays dominant strategies) is a refinement of NE (everyone plays mutual best-responses); NE need not be unique or “good” (prisoner’s dilemma).
  • Strategic commitments can select an equilibrium in coordination games.
  • In infinitely repeated games, patient players (low \( r \)) sustain cooperation with grim trigger strategies; finite games unravel via the endgame problem.
