The Quick Ideas

Part A · Multiple Choice

Q1–4: Read, Then Reveal

Q1. Which change shifts market demand for boba to the left?

(d) A cheaper rival chain (a substitute) and falling income for a normal good both cut demand. A heat wave shifts it right; a lower boba price is a move along the curve.

Q2. A \(\$60\) nonrefundable ticket — you’d now rather stay home. How does the \(\$60\) factor in?

(a) It’s a sunk cost — unrecoverable and the same either way — so it’s irrelevant. Compare only tonight’s marginal benefit vs. cost, and stay home.

Q3. \( |\varepsilon| = 0.8 \). Does a 10% price hike raise total revenue?

(a) Demand is inelastic \( (|\varepsilon|<1) \): price up 10%, quantity down only about 8%, so revenue rises.

Q4. For \( P = 20 - 2Q \), which statement about \( |\varepsilon| \) is right?

(d) All of them. \( |\varepsilon| = \tfrac12\cdot\tfrac{P}{Q} \) gives \( 3,\ 1,\ \tfrac13 \) at \( Q = 2.5,\ 5,\ 7.5 \) — elasticity falls as you slide down the line.

Part A · Multiple Choice

Q5–8: Read, Then Reveal

Q5. Priya: \( MC=\$3.20 \), \( AVC=\$2.00 \), \( ATC=\$4.00 \) at 200 loaves; price \(\$2.80\). Do what?

(d) \( P=\$2.80 > AVC \), so operate despite the loss. But \( MC=\$3.20 > P \): she’s making too many — cut output until \( MC \) falls to \(\$2.80\).

Q6. A monopolist’s \( MC \) rises by exactly \(\$6\). Raise price by how much?

(c) By less than \(\$6\). \( MR \) has twice the slope of demand, so the new price rises by only about half the cost increase.

Q7. A per-unit tax’s deadweight loss is best described as…

(c) The value of mutually beneficial trades that no longer happen because of the tax wedge. Revenue is a transfer, not a loss.

Q8. Which is a public good (non-rival and non-excludable)?

(d) National defense. A sandwich is private; a fishery is a common resource; streaming and a toll road are club goods (excludable).

Part B · True / False

Q9: Some Right, Some Not

(a) A decrease in a good’s price shifts its demand curve left. False that’s a move along the curve.
(b) A linear demand curve is more elastic at higher prices (upper portion). True
(c) A good with income elasticity \( < 0 \) is a normal good. False negative \( \Rightarrow \) inferior.
(d) For linear demand, \( MR \) shares the intercept but has twice the slope. True
(e) A monopolist sets \( MR=MC \) for quantity, then charges the highest price demand allows. True
(f) A competitive firm should shut down whenever it makes a loss. False only if \( P < AVC \).
(g) In long-run competitive equilibrium, profit is zero and \( P = \min ATC \). True
(h) A per-unit tax’s DWL equals the revenue it raises. False revenue is a transfer, not the loss.

Part B · Externalities

Q10: Runoff & Herd Immunity

The question
A fertilizer plant’s runoff harms a downstream fishery (a negative externality in production); a vaccinated student protects classmates (a positive externality in consumption). For each: over- or under-produce? Name the corrective policy.
Step 1 — Runoff: over-produce
External harm means \( MSC > MPC \). The market trades where price meets private cost, ignoring the harm, so it over-produces relative to the efficient quantity (where price meets \( MSC \)).
Step 2 — Vaccines: under-produce
External benefit means \( MSB > MPB \). Buyers consume where price meets private benefit, ignoring the gain to others, so the market under-produces.
Step 3 — The fix
Negative externality \( \Rightarrow \) a Pigouvian tax on the polluter (lowers \( Q \) to \( Q^* \)). Positive externality \( \Rightarrow \) a subsidy to consumers (raises \( Q \) to \( Q^* \)).

Part B · Public Goods

Q11: No Price, No Producer

The question
A town weighs a Fourth-of-July fireworks display. Why is it non-rival and non-excludable? Why would a private firm under-provide it? Name a fix — and classify a congested toll road.
Step 1 — Why it’s a public good
Non-rival: one person watching doesn’t reduce anyone else’s view. Non-excludable: you can’t fence off the night sky to stop non-payers.
Step 2 — The free-rider problem
Since non-payers can’t be excluded, everyone waits for someone else to pay. A private firm can’t collect enough to cover costs, so it under-provides (or never provides) — “no price, no producer.”
Step 3 — A fix, and the toll road
Fix: government provision funded by taxes. A congested toll road is excludable (the toll) but rival (each car slows the rest) \( \Rightarrow \) it’s a private good.

Q12 · 14 points

Avocado Toast Economics

The weekly market for avocados is perfectly competitive, with

\[ Q_D = 120 - 10P, \qquad Q_S = 20P \]

\( Q \) is in thousands of avocados; \( P \) is dollars per avocado.

Your tasks
  • (a) Find the equilibrium price \( P^* \) and quantity \( Q^* \).
  • (b) Compute \( CS \), \( PS \), and welfare \( W = CS + PS \); sketch and label the graph.
  • (c) A heat wave cuts supply to \( Q_S = 20P - 60 \). Find the new equilibrium; say what happens to \( CS \) and to \( W \).

Q12 (a) · Equilibrium

Set Quantity Demanded = Quantity Supplied

Step 1 — Equate the two curves
\[ 120 - 10P = 20P \;\Longrightarrow\; 30P = 120 \;\Longrightarrow\; P^* = \$4 \]
Step 2 — Back out the quantity
\[ Q^* = 20P^* = 20(4) = 80 \text{ thousand avocados} \]

Equilibrium: \( P^* = \$4 \), \( Q^* = 80 \) (thousand).

Q12 (b) · Surplus & Welfare

Consumer Surplus, Producer Surplus, Welfare

Demand choke price (where \( Q_D = 0 \)) is \( P = \$12 \); supply passes through the origin.

Consumer surplus
\[ CS = \tfrac12 Q^*(P_{\text{choke}} - P^*) = \tfrac12(80)(12-4) = \$320 \]
Producer surplus
\[ PS = \tfrac12 Q^* P^* = \tfrac12(80)(4) = \$160 \]
Total welfare
\[ W = CS + PS = 320 + 160 = \$480 \text{ thousand} \]

Q12 (c) · Supply Shock

The Heat Wave: Supply Falls

Step 1 — New equilibrium with \( Q_S = 20P - 60 \)
\[ 120 - 10P = 20P - 60 \;\Longrightarrow\; 180 = 30P \;\Longrightarrow\; P = \$6 \] \[ Q = 120 - 10(6) = 60 \text{ thousand} \]
Step 2 — Direction of \( CS \) and \( W \)
Price rises (\(\$4 \to \$6\)) and quantity falls (\(80 \to 60\)). Consumers pay more and buy less, so \( CS \) falls; fewer mutually beneficial trades occur (and real costs are higher), so total welfare \( W \) falls too.

Q13 · 8 points

Three Elasticities

Your tasks — show the formula each time
  • (a) Demand \( Q = 60 - 5P \). Find the own-price point elasticity \( |\varepsilon| \) at \( P = 8 \). Elastic, unit-elastic, or inelastic?
  • (b) Income falls 5%; instant-noodle purchases rise 2%. Income elasticity? Normal or inferior?
  • (c) Coffee’s price rises 20%; tea sales rise 8%. Cross-price elasticity? Complements or substitutes?

Q13 · Worked Answers

One Formula, Three Uses

(a) Own-price point elasticity
\( \left|\tfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P}\right| = 5 \); at \( P = 8 \), \( Q = 60 - 5(8) = 20 \). \[ |\varepsilon| = \left|\tfrac{\Delta Q}{\Delta P}\right|\cdot\tfrac{P}{Q} = 5\cdot\tfrac{8}{20} = 2 \;>\; 1 \;\Rightarrow\; \textbf{elastic} \]
(b) Income elasticity
\[ \varepsilon_I = \frac{\%\Delta Q}{\%\Delta I} = \frac{+2\%}{-5\%} = -0.4 \;<\; 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \textbf{inferior good} \] Income falls yet she buys more — the hallmark of an inferior good.
(c) Cross-price elasticity
\[ \varepsilon_{\text{tea, coffee}} = \frac{\%\Delta Q_{\text{tea}}}{\%\Delta P_{\text{coffee}}} = \frac{+8\%}{+20\%} = 0.4 \;>\; 0 \;\Rightarrow\; \textbf{substitutes} \] Positive sign: pricier coffee sends buyers toward tea.

Q14 · 16 points

The Only Food Truck

A food truck is the sole seller of lunch — a monopoly. It faces

\[ P = 100 - 2Q, \qquad MC = \$20 \text{ (constant, no fixed cost)} \]

\( Q \) is meals per day (in hundreds); \( P \) is dollars per meal.

Your tasks
  • (a) Derive \( MR \); find \( Q_M \), \( P_M \), and profit \( \pi \).
  • (b) Find the competitive \( Q_c, P_c \) (where \( P = MC \)); then the monopoly \( CS \) and \( PS \).
  • (c) Compute the deadweight loss vs. competition, and draw the labeled graph.

Q14 (a) · The Monopoly Optimum

Marginal Revenue, Then \( MR = MC \)

Step 1 — Build \( MR \) from revenue
\( R = PQ = (100 - 2Q)Q = 100Q - 2Q^2 \), so \[ MR = 100 - 4Q \quad(\text{same intercept as } D,\ \text{twice the slope}) \]
Step 2 — Set \( MR = MC \) for quantity
\[ 100 - 4Q = 20 \;\Longrightarrow\; 4Q = 80 \;\Longrightarrow\; Q_M = 20 \]
Step 3 — Price off demand, then profit
\[ P_M = 100 - 2(20) = \$60, \qquad \pi = (P_M - MC)Q_M = (60-20)(20) = 800 \] (With \( Q \) in hundreds and no fixed cost, \( \pi = 800 \) hundreds \( = \$80{,}000 \)/day.)

Q14 (b) · Benchmark & Surplus

Competitive Benchmark, Monopoly Surplus

Step 1 — Competitive outcome \( (P = MC) \)
\[ 100 - 2Q = 20 \;\Longrightarrow\; Q_c = 40, \qquad P_c = \$20 \]
Step 2 — Consumer surplus under monopoly (choke \(\$100\))
\[ CS = \tfrac12 Q_M(P_{\text{choke}} - P_M) = \tfrac12(20)(100 - 60) = 400 \]
Step 3 — Producer surplus under monopoly
\[ PS = (P_M - MC)Q_M = (60 - 20)(20) = 800 \quad(=\pi,\ \text{since } MC \text{ constant, no fixed cost}) \]

Q14 (c) · Deadweight Loss

The Cost of Monopoly Power

The triangle between \( Q_M \) and \( Q_c \)
\[ DWL = \tfrac12 (Q_c - Q_M)(P_M - MC) \] \[ = \tfrac12 (40 - 20)(60 - 20) = 400 \]

The monopoly makes only 20 (hundred) meals instead of 40. On units 20–40, buyers value lunch above the \(\$20\) cost of making it — those gains from trade simply vanish. That lost surplus is the red triangle.

Q15 · 12 points

A Dime a Dozen

The generic-bagel market is perfectly competitive. Every identical firm has

\[ TC(q) = 2q^2 + 50, \quad MC(q) = 4q, \quad AVC = 2q, \quad ATC = \tfrac{50}{q} + 2q \]

\( q \) is in hundreds of bagels/day. Market demand: \( Q_D = 3000 - 25P \).

Your tasks
  • (a) At \( P = \$40 \): find \( q \) and profit. Operate or shut down? Compare \( P \) to \( AVC \).
  • (b) With free entry/exit, find the long-run price and each firm’s output. [min \( ATC \) at \( q = 5 \).] Profit?
  • (c) At the long-run price, find the total market quantity and the number of firms.

Q15 (a) · Short Run

Output, Profit, and the Shutdown Check

Step 1 — A competitive firm sets \( P = MC \)
\[ 40 = 4q \;\Longrightarrow\; q = 10 \]
Step 2 — Profit
\[ \pi = TR - TC = 40(10) - \big[2(10)^2 + 50\big] = 400 - 250 = \$150 \]
Step 3 — Operate or shut down?
\( AVC(10) = 2(10) = \$20 < P = \$40 \): the price covers average variable cost, so operate — indeed the firm earns a positive short-run profit.

Q15 (b) · Long Run

Free Entry Competes Profit Away

Step 1 — In the long run, \( P = \min ATC \)
With minimum \( ATC \) at \( q = 5 \): \[ ATC(5) = \tfrac{50}{5} + 2(5) = 10 + 10 = \$20 \]
Step 2 — The long-run equilibrium
\[ P_{LR} = \$20, \qquad q_{LR} = 5, \qquad \pi = \$0 \] Each surviving firm just breaks even — zero economic profit.

Q15 (c) · The Whole Market

Market Quantity and the Number of Firms

Step 1 — Market quantity at \( P_{LR} = \$20 \)
\[ Q = 3000 - 25(20) = 2500 \]
Step 2 — Number of firms
\[ \#\text{ firms} = \frac{Q}{q_{LR}} = \frac{2500}{5} = 500 \]

Q16 · 19 points (+5 E.C.)

Making APT Decisions

APT Press prints magazines in a perfectly competitive market. Demand and identical-firm costs:

\[ Q_D = 64 - 2P_D, \qquad TC(q) = 2q^2 + 2q + 32, \qquad MC(q) = 4q + 2 \]

\( Q_D \) in thousands of copies; \( q \) in tens of copies. Current price they take as given: \(\$10\).

Your tasks
  • (a) At \(\$10\), find APT’s profit-maximizing \( q \).   (b) Market quantity and number of firms.
  • (c) Profit is negative — short-run or long-run?   (d) Do firms enter or exit?
  • (e) In long-run equilibrium: price, APT’s \( q \), market quantity, number of firms.
  • (f) If APT becomes a monopolist: profit-maximizing \( Q \) and \( P \).   (g) [E.C.] Elasticity there, and why never price inelastic.

Q16 (a)–(d) · Short Run

Where APT Stands Today

(a) Profit-maximizing output — \( MR = P = MC \)
\[ 4q + 2 = 10 \;\Longrightarrow\; 4q = 8 \;\Longrightarrow\; q = 2 \text{ (tens of copies)} \]
(b) Market quantity and number of firms
\( Q_D = 64 - 2(10) = 44 \) (thousand). With each firm at \( q = 2 \): \[ N = \frac{Q_D}{q} = \frac{44}{2} = 22 \text{ (hundred)} \;\Rightarrow\; 2{,}200 \text{ firms} \]
(c)–(d) Losses, so…
\( \pi = 10(2) - \big[2(2)^2 + 2(2) + 32\big] = 20 - 44 = -24 < 0 \). Negative profit \( \Rightarrow \) this is a short-run equilibrium, and as the market adjusts some firms will exit.

Q16 (e) · Long Run

Settling Into Long-Run Equilibrium

Step 1 — Zero profit means \( MC = ATC \)
With \( ATC = \tfrac{TC}{q} = 2q + 2 + \tfrac{32}{q} \): \[ 2q + 2 + \tfrac{32}{q} = 4q + 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; 2q = \tfrac{32}{q} \;\Longrightarrow\; q^2 = 16 \;\Longrightarrow\; q = 4 \text{ (tens)} \]
Step 2 — Long-run price, market quantity
\[ P = MC(4) = 4(4) + 2 = \$18, \qquad Q_D = 64 - 2(18) = 28 \text{ (thousand)} \]
Step 3 — Number of firms
\[ N = \frac{Q_D}{q} = \frac{28}{4} = 7 \text{ (hundred)} \;\Rightarrow\; 700 \text{ firms} \] Consistent with (c)–(d): from 2,200 down to 700 — most firms exited.

Q16 (f) · APT as Monopolist

Last Firm Standing

Step 1 — Invert demand, build \( MR \)
\( Q_D = 64 - 2P \Rightarrow P_D = 32 - \tfrac12 Q_D \), so \( MR = 32 - Q \).
Step 2 — Set \( MR = MC \)
\[ 32 - Q = 4Q + 2 \;\Longrightarrow\; 30 = 5Q \;\Longrightarrow\; Q^* = 6 \text{ (thousand)}, \quad P^* = 32 - \tfrac12(6) = \$29 \]
Step 3 — Profit (the +2 extra)
\( TR = 29(6) = 174 \), \( TC = 2(6)^2 + 2(6) + 32 = 116 \), so \( \pi = 174 - 116 = \$58 \) thousand.

Q16 (g) · Extra Credit

Why a Monopolist Avoids the Inelastic Region

Step 1 — Elasticity at \( (P^*, Q^*) = (29, 6) \)
\( Q_D = 64 - 2P \Rightarrow \tfrac{dQ}{dP} = -2 \), so \[ \varepsilon_D = \frac{dQ}{dP}\cdot\frac{P}{Q} = -2\cdot\frac{29}{6} = -\frac{29}{3} \approx -9.67 \quad(\text{elastic}) \]
Step 2 — The intuition
On the inelastic portion, \( MR < 0 \): cutting quantity (raising price) would raise total revenue and lower cost. So the firm can always increase profit by moving left — until \( MR = MC > 0 \), which lies on the elastic portion. A monopolist never optimally prices where demand is inelastic.

Q17 · 10 points

A Spoonful of Sugar Tax

The sugary-soda market (in thousands of cans) is perfectly competitive, with

\[ Q_D = 120 - 20P, \qquad Q_S = 40P \]

The city imposes a \(\$0.60\) per-can tax collected from sellers.

Your task — one big, fully-labeled diagram
Sketch the supply-and-demand diagram with the tax, and clearly show and label:
  • the curves \( D \) and \( S \), and the pre-tax equilibrium \( P^*, Q^* \);
  • the price buyers pay \( P_b \), the price sellers keep \( P_s \), and the after-tax quantity \( Q_{\text{tax}} \);
  • the four areas — \( CS \), \( PS \), government tax revenue, and \( DWL \) — shaded, labeled, and with their dollar values.

Q17 · Setup

The Numbers You’ll Put on the Graph

Step 1 — Pre-tax equilibrium \( (Q_D = Q_S) \)
\[ 120 - 20P = 40P \;\Longrightarrow\; 60P = 120 \;\Longrightarrow\; P^* = \$2, \quad Q^* = 40(2) = 80 \]
Step 2 — The tax wedge \( (P_b = P_s + 0.60) \)
\[ 120 - 20(P_s + 0.60) = 40P_s \;\Longrightarrow\; 108 = 60P_s \;\Longrightarrow\; P_s = \$1.80,\; P_b = \$2.40 \] \[ Q_{\text{tax}} = 40(1.80) = 72 \]
Step 3 — Incidence & the choke price
Consumers pay \(\$0.40\) more, producers \(\$0.20\) less \( \Rightarrow \) consumers bear \( \tfrac23 \), producers \( \tfrac13 \) (demand is the more inelastic side). The demand choke price is \(\$6\), where \( Q_D = 0 \).

Q17 · The Four Areas

Surplus, Revenue, and Loss

Consumer surplus
\[ CS = \tfrac12 Q_{\text{tax}}(6 - P_b) = \tfrac12(72)(3.60) = 129.6 \]
Producer surplus
\[ PS = \tfrac12 Q_{\text{tax}} P_s = \tfrac12(72)(1.80) = 64.8 \]
Government revenue
\[ t \times Q_{\text{tax}} = 0.60 \times 72 = 43.2 \]
Deadweight loss
\[ DWL = \tfrac12 t (Q^* - Q_{\text{tax}}) = \tfrac12(0.60)(8) = 2.4 \]
Sanity check — it all adds up
\( 129.6 + 64.8 + 43.2 + 2.4 = 240 \), exactly the pre-tax total surplus (all figures in thousands of dollars).

Q17 · The Finished Diagram

One Tax, Four Regions

The \(\$0.60\) wedge separates \( P_b \) (buyers) from \( P_s \) (sellers); the quantity falls from \( Q^* = 80 \) to \( Q_{\text{tax}} = 72 \). Areas are in thousands of dollars.

You’ve Reviewed All of Classes 1–6

The through-line of every problem: total surplus. Competition maximizes it; monopoly, taxes, and market failures each open a gap.

  • Equilibrium, \( CS \), \( PS \), and welfare — and how a supply shock moves them.
  • The three elasticities, and reading them off a demand curve.
  • Monopoly: \( MR = MC \), the markup, and the deadweight-loss triangle.
  • Perfect competition: the shutdown rule and the drive to \( P = \min ATC \).
