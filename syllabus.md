## Course description

My hope is that these four weeks give you the foundational knowledge of microeconomics
and confer on you the set of expectations a college instructor has for your work. I
expect everyone to **show up**, **be engaged**, **not be afraid to ask questions**,
**not be afraid to answer questions and share your thought process**, and **try your
best**.

We will learn some of the fundamental mathematical tools needed to construct reasoned
arguments, but — to repeat — the focus of this course is *not* to develop mastery in
mathematics.

## How to succeed in this class

This Experience introduces two fundamental ideas in the study of microeconomics:

1. **Decision-making on the margin**, and what this decision-rule implies for
   individuals and firms; and
2. **Market competition under perfect information**, and how firms and individuals
   strategize in environments with *imperfect* information.

To guarantee your success, do everything you can to:

1. Consistently stay on top of all the new class content.
2. Study and attempt the practice questions.
3. Work *with each other* to do #2.
4. **Ask for help from Jasmine, Aubrey, and/or me — early and quickly.** We are here to
   help you learn the content and prepare you for the real ECON 101.
5. Use the quizzes to confirm to yourself that you *are* learning the core concepts.

> As a matter of principle (and fact): grades become *much* less important over your
> life. Trust me on this as someone rapidly reaching the end of their education journey.
> Use your quiz and exam scores as an indication of how much you're learning — and to
> figure out what you must learn going forward.

  interesting and contains some principles of economics (no topic restrictions — the goal
  is to build the habit of reading the news).
- The grading rubric is below.

## Policies & expectations {#policies}

**Attendance & participation.** Participating in conversations makes all of us more
informed — I benefit a lot from listening, understanding, asking clarifying questions,
and raising thoughts. *I strongly encourage everyone to show up, participate, and
question.* The self-interested case is twofold: you'll learn from the experience (the
whole point of RSC), and — as a future business professional — you are your own personal
brand. Now is a good time to start building it; shirking tasks gets noticed and won't
help that brand.

**Laptop & phone policy.** To keep your attention on the important thing — our class —
please avoid distracting yourself with unnecessary electronic devices. For evidence on
the costs of multitasking, see Bellur et al. (2015), who find that in-class multitasking
leads to significantly lower learning performance
([link](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0747563215004677)).

**Office hours & contact.** Office hours are Wednesdays & Thursdays, 7–9pm. If those
times don't work, email me with **"RSC" in the subject line** and include (a) when you'd
like to meet and (b) the specific questions or challenges you're facing.
